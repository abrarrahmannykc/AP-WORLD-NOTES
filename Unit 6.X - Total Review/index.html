    AP World History Unit 6 Comprehensive Review: Consequences of Industrialization (1750–1900)¶

    1. The Core Motives: Rationales for Imperialism¶

    The Industrial Revolution created an enormous shift in global power dynamics. Wealthy industrial nations developed core ideological, cultural, and political rationales to justify their aggressive territorial expansion.

    • The Core Drivers:

      • Cultural and Racial Ideologies: Concepts like Social Darwinism distorted biological evolutionary theory to argue that white Europeans were racially superior and destined to conquer "less fit" societies.

      • The "Civilizing Mission": Western powers asserted an obligation to spread features of modern civilization—including technology, industrial infrastructure, medicine, and Western law—to non-industrialized populations.

      • Religious Conversion: Christian missionaries traveled extensively to set up schools and churches, aiming to religiously convert indigenous populations.

      • Nationalism: Acquiring overseas colonies became a prime competitive status symbol for European states, the United States, and Meiji Japan to project global power.

    2. The Methods: State Expansion and Imperial Shifts¶

    Imperialism shifted away from the early modern model of loose, coastal trading posts toward direct territorial conquest, political absorption, and settler colonization.

    • The Institutional Shifts:

      • The Belgian Congo: King Leopold II originally ran the Congo Basin as a brutal, private corporate monopoly. Following global outcry over rubber harvesting atrocities, the Belgian government stepped in and transitioned it into a direct state colony.

      • British India: Control shifted cleanly from the private corporate military force of the British East India Company to direct British Crown governance (the British Raj) following the widespread Indian Rebellion of 1857.

      • The Dutch East Indies: The bankrupt Dutch East India Company (VOC) was formally dissolved, transferring direct political control of Indonesia over to the official Dutch government.

    • New Forms of Expansion:

      • The Scramble for Africa: European powers used diplomacy (the Berlin Conference) and industrial warfare to slice up 90% of the African continent into states.

      • Settler Colonies: Empires established settler colonies—such as the British in New Zealand—where large waves of white European families permanently relocated to replace or dominate the indigenous population.

      • Regional Conquests: The United States, Russia, and Meiji Japan rapidly expanded their immediate land holdings by conquering and settling neighboring indigenous territories.

    3. The Resistance: Indigenous Pushback¶

    Indigenous populations across the globe actively countered imperial expansion through direct armed conflicts, political organizing, and supernatural or religious resistance movements.

    Major Anti-Colonial Direct Resistance¶

    • The 1857 Rebellion in India: Sepoys revolted over religious insults to Hindu and Muslim dietary traditions, triggering a massive regional anti-colonial war that forced the British state to take direct control of governance.

    • The Yaa Asantewaa War: In West Africa, Queen Mother Yaa Asantewaa led the Asante Kingdom in a fierce military defense to protect their sacred Golden Stool from British imperial capture.

    • The Balkan Independent States: Diverse ethnic populations in the Balkan peninsula organized national resistance movements to break away from the declining land-based Ottoman Empire, forming new, independent states.

    Religious and Spiritual Resistance¶

    When confronted with superior Western industrial weapons, several indigenous societies turned to spiritual movements to protect their sovereignty:

    • The Ghost Dance (USA): Native American Plains nations united in a sacred dance ritual believed to make white settlers disappear and bring back their ancestral lands.

    • The Xhosa Cattle-Killing Movement (South Africa): Faced with British expansion and livestock disease, the Xhosa mass-slaughtered their own cattle based on a prophecy that ancestral spirits would arise to sweep the British into the ocean.

    • The Mahdist Wars (Sudan): An Islamic cleric declared himself the Mahdi and led a fundamentalist holy war to purge British and Egyptian forces from East Africa.

    4. The Economics: Resource Extraction and Imperial Traps¶

    The global economic structure was fundamentally organized to serve Western industrial manufacturing advantage, trapping weaker nations into highly exploitative relationships.

    Export Economies vs. Economic Imperialism¶

    • Export Economies (Raw Resource Extraction): Industrialized cities required massive agricultural and raw inputs. Non-industrialized regions were converted into specialized resource exporters, abandoning self-sufficient farming to harvest single commodities.

      • Cotton: Mass-cultivated in Egypt and South Asia to feed European textile mills.

      • Rubber and Palm Oil: Extracted from the Congo basin, Amazon, and West Africa to act as tires, machine belts, and vital industrial lubricants.

      • Guano: Mined in Peru and Chile to serve as fertilizer for high-yield farming.

      • Meat: Exported from Argentina via new commercial refrigerated steamships.

    • Economic Imperialism (Financial Domination): Rather than claiming land, Western countries used corporate monopolies, debt leverage, and forced trade treaties to subvert sovereign foreign markets.

      • The Opium Wars: Britain and France smuggled South Asian opium into China to intentionally correct a trade deficit. They used naval victories to force open Chinese markets and carve out exclusionary economic Spheres of Influence.

      • British Firms in Argentina: British capitalists fully financed and controlled the modernized Port of Buenos Aires and local rail systems, gaining total economic advantage over Argentina's export profits without ever establishing a formal political colony.

    5. The Demographics: Causes and Effects of Global Migration¶

    Industrialization and imperial networks created an era of unprecedented global migration, shifting demographics and sparking deep social and political changes around the world.

    Causes of Migration (Push/Pull Dynamics)¶

    • Push Factors: Extreme population pressures, land shortages, poverty, and local resource destruction (e.g., the Great Famine in Ireland or colonial displacement in India).

    • Pull Factors: Rapid global urbanization and industrial factory jobs drew millions of laborers to major cities.

    • The Tech Catalyst: New transportation systems—such as commercial steamships and cross-continental railroads—made seasonal and long-distance intercontinental relocation highly accessible and affordable.

    Effects of Migration¶

    • Social Impact on Home Countries: Because physical labor demands meant that migrants were predominantly male, women left behind took on dynamic new roles. They assumed complete responsibility for local family finances, agricultural fields, and domestic businesses formerly managed by men.

    • Cultural Transmission (Ethnic Enclaves): Migrants settled closely together in receiving nations to preserve their heritage, establishing unique ethnic enclaves.

      • Examples: Chinese communities throughout Southeast Asia and the Americas; Indian enclaves across Southern and East Africa; and Italian neighborhoods in Argentina.

    • Political Resistance and Xenophobia: Native populations and receiving governments frequently reacted with deep racial prejudice and economic fear. States passed strict discriminatory laws to purposefully control and block non-white immigration across their borders.

      • Examples: The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) in the United States and the explicitly restrictive White Australia Policy (1901).

    💡 What is Most Important for the Exam?¶

    1. The Continuity of Exploitation: Connect Unit 6 to the Industrial Revolution. Empires didn't conquer just for pride; they conquered because factories had a systemic, insatiable need for raw materials and captive consumer markets.

    2. Supernatural Defiance: Remember that indigenous resistance wasn't just military; movements like the Ghost Dance and Xhosa Cattle-Killing show how deep cultural and spiritual beliefs were used to try and combat superior Western technology.

    3. The Migration Loop: Industrialization created the technology that allowed mass migration (steamships), the jobs that pulled migrants (railroads/plantations), the demographic shifts at home (women taking power), and the legal backlashes abroad (Chinese Exclusion Act).
