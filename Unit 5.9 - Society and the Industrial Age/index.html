    AP World History Unit 5 Review Notes: Society and the Industrial Age (Topic 5.9)¶

    Topic 5.9 looks at the profound social transformations brought about by global capitalism and industrialization, focusing on the emergence of entirely new social classes, shifting demographic roles within families, and the massive challenges of rapid urbanization.

    1. The Realignment of Social Classes¶

    Industrialization completely shattered old feudal hierarchies (which were based on land-owning nobles and peasants) and created an entirely new class structure rooted in capital and factory labor.

    • The Industrial Working Class: A vast new social class developed to staff the factories, mines, and transportation networks. These families relied purely on wage-earning jobs and faced grueling, unsafe work environments.

    • The Middle Class: A new, prosperous class of professionals also developed, consisting of factory managers, doctors, lawyers, accountants, and merchants. They benefited immensely from the profits of industrial capitalism and enjoyed an increased standard of living.

    2. Shifting Family Dynamics and Gender Roles¶

    Prior to the factory system, families typically worked together in agricultural or domestic settings (like the cottage industry). Industrialization fractured this by moving work outside of the home, changing family dynamics based on class.

    • Working-Class Families: Because working-class factory wages were low, women and often children in these families typically held wage-earning jobs to supplement their families' income. They worked long hours in textile mills or coal mines alongside men, but usually for a fraction of the pay.

    • Middle-Class Families: Middle-class women did not have the same economic demands to satisfy. Instead, their lives became increasingly limited to roles in the household or roles focused on child development.

    • The "Cult of Domesticity": This middle-class separation of roles led to an ideology that idealized the home as a peaceful, moral sanctuary where women were expected to manage domestic life, care for children, and provide emotional support, while men operated exclusively in the competitive public sphere of business and politics.

    3. The Challenges of Rapid Urbanization¶

    The rapid influx of workers moving from rural areas to cities to work in factories contributed to unprecedented, global urbanization. Because cities grew far too quickly for municipal governments to keep pace, global capitalism at times led to a variety of severe challenges:

    • Environmental Degradation: Factories belched thick coal smoke into the air, while industrial waste and raw sewage poisoned local water supplies, leading to rampant pollution.

    • Public Health Crises: Overcrowded neighborhoods lacked proper sanitation or clean water systems, making them breeding grounds for deadly epidemic diseases like cholera and typhus.

    • Poverty and Crime: Desperate economic conditions and a lack of social safety nets led to widespread poverty and increased crime rates in slum neighborhoods.

    • Infrastructure Deficits: Cities suffered from severe housing shortages and highly insufficient infrastructure (such as a lack of paved roads, transit, trash removal, and sewage systems) to accommodate the massive urban growth.

    What is Most Important for the Exam?¶

    1. Class-Based Gender Roles: Expect DBQ (Document-Based Question) or SAQ prompts to test you on the contrast between working-class and middle-class women. Working-class women worked in factories out of economic necessity, while middle-class women were bound by the social expectations of the "Cult of Domesticity".

    2. Urbanization as a Problem-Generator: Always connect the rapid growth of factories (Topic 5.3) to the negative social symptoms in Topic 5.9. The pollution, housing shortages, and public health crises are the direct human cost of unchecked industrial capitalism, which is exactly why the reform movements of Topic 5.8 emerged.

    3. The Micro vs. Macro View: On the macro scale, industrial capitalism increased the overall standard of living and choice of consumer goods (Topic 5.7). On the micro scale, however, it degraded daily life for the urban working class via tenements, pollution, and fragmented family structures (Topic 5.9). Be ready to use this nuance in an essay to show complex historical thinking.
