    Unit 5.1 and 5.2 From Enlightenment to Revolutions

    1. The Core Connection: From Theory to Action¶

    The transition from the Enlightenment to the age of revolutions relies on a clean cause-and-effect loop:

       [ UNIT 5.1: THE IDEAS ]                      [ UNIT 5.2: THE ACTIONS ]
Philosophers attack absolute rule         Revolutionaries use those ideas
and outline "Natural Rights."       ───>  to draft foundational documents
(Locke, Voltaire, Rousseau)              and justify throwing off empires.
    • The Justification for Rebellion: Prior to this era, rebelling against a king was seen as a sin against God (due to the Divine Right of Kings). The Enlightenment changed the rules: if a government fails to protect its citizens' natural rights, the Social Contract is broken, and the people have a legitimate right to rebel.

    2. Topic 5.1 Notes: The Enlightenment (The Ideals)¶

    The Enlightenment was an intellectual movement that applied empiricism and reason to human relationships, politics, and religion, questioning centuries-old established traditions.

    The Core Philosophers and Concepts¶

    • John Locke & Natural Rights: Locke argued that all humans are born with the rights to life, liberty, and property. He pioneered the Social Contract—the idea that people willingly surrender some freedom to a government in exchange for the protection of their rights.

    • Voltaire & Individual Freedoms: Advocated fiercely for religious tolerance, free speech, and the clean separation of church and state.

    • Montesquieu & Separation of Powers: Argued that to prevent tyranny, government authority must be fractured into separate executive, legislative, and judicial branches.

    The Social Ripple Effects: Reform Movements¶

    Enlightenment thought didn't just target absolute monarchs; it directly sparked movements challenging deeply rooted social hierarchies:

    • Emergent Feminism: Women used Enlightenment principles to demand political, social, and gender equality.

      • Mary Wollstonecraft: Wrote A Vindication of the Rights of Woman, arguing that women are not naturally inferior to men, but appear that way only due to a lack of education.

      • Olympe de Gouges: Penned the Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen during the French Revolution to challenge male revolutionary hypocrisy.

      • Seneca Falls Convention (1888): An early landmark meeting in the U.S. organized by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott to fight for women’s suffrage (the right to vote).

    • Abolition of Slavery & End of Serfdom: The universal claim that all humans possess natural rights generated massive moral friction against chattel slavery and feudal serfdom, leading to their gradual legal collapse across the Atlantic world during the 19th century.

    3. Topic 5.2 Notes: Nationalism and Revolutions (The Results)¶

    Discontent with monarchist and imperial rule, combined with new Enlightenment ideologies, triggered an intense wave of global revolutions from 1750 to 1900, culminating in the birth of modern nation-states.

    Ideals Transplanted into Foundational Documents¶

    Revolutionaries didn't just fight; they put their Enlightenment rationales in writing to prove their causes were just:

    • The American Declaration of Independence: Directly plagiarized John Locke, declaring it a self-evident truth that all men possess unalienable rights to Life, Liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness.

    • The French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen: Drafted during the collapse of the French absolute monarchy, this document guaranteed civic equality, liberty, and security to all French citizens.

    • Simón Bolívar’s "Letter from Jamaica": Written on the eve of the Latin American revolutions, Bolívar used Enlightenment rhetoric to outline his vision for independence from Spanish imperial rule and the defense of democratic ideals.

    The Atlantic Revolutions and Anti-Colonial Uprisings¶

    • The American Revolution (1776): Colonial subjects in British North America successfully revolted against tax overreach and imperial control, creating the world's first modern constitutional republic.

    • The French Revolution (1789): An internal explosion driven by the lower class (the Third Estate) against royal extravagance and aristocratic privilege. It resulted in the execution of Louis XVI and a total transformation of European politics.

    • The Haitian Revolution (1791): Led by Toussaint Louverture, enslaved populations on the French sugar colony of Saint-Domingue rose up. It stands as the only completely successful slave revolt in world history, creating the first independent Black republic.

    • Latin American Independence Movements: Creoles (wealthy American-born Spanish elites like Simón Bolívar) led a series of successful wars against Spain, establishing independent republics across South and Central America.

    4. The Rise of Global Nationalism¶

    The ultimate byproduct of these revolutions was the explosive rise of nationalism—a new sense of common identity shared by a population based on a common language, history, religion, and social customs.

    Governments intentionally harnessed nationalism to unify populations or foster intense loyalty to the state.

    Types of Nationalism in Action¶

    • Unification Movements: Fragmented regional states discovered a common identity and merged into massive nation-states (e.g., the complex political unifications of Germany and Italy in the late 1800s).

    • Anti-Imperial Separations: Ethnic groups living under sprawling, land-based multi-ethnic empires organized national movements to break away and win independence.

      • Balkan Nationalisms: Groups like the Greeks, Serbs, and Bulgarians launched rebellions to tear away from the weakening Ottoman Empire.

    • Resistance to Western Encroachment:

      • Maori Nationalism: The indigenous Maori of New Zealand developed unified political and spiritual movements to resist British land seizure and state dominance.

    What is Most Important for the Exam?¶

    1. The Continuity of Documents: The AP Exam loves to compare the documents mentioned in Unit 5.2 (Declaration of Independence, Rights of Man, Jamaica Letter) to prove that the Enlightenment (Unit 5.1) was a global, shared intellectual baseline.

    2. The Creole Motivation: Understand why Latin American revolutions happened. Creoles wanted independence from Spain to gain economic freedom, but they used Enlightenment vocabulary to justify their rebellion while keeping themselves at the top of the social hierarchy.

    3. The Radicality of Haiti: Recognize that while France and America talked about liberty, they both maintained slavery. Haiti was the only revolution that took Enlightenment ideals to their logical, most radical conclusion: total human emancipation.
