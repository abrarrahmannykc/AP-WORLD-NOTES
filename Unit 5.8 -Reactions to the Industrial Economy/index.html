    AP World History Unit 5 Review Notes: Reactions to the Industrial Economy (Topic 5.8)¶

    Topic 5.8 explores the widespread social, political, and economic backlash against the hardships of industrial capitalism between 1750 and 1900. As the factory system transformed daily life, workers, intellectuals, and non-Western states launched major reform movements or advanced radical new visions of society.

    1. Domestic Reforms and Labor Unions¶

    In heavily industrialized Western states, the brutal realities of early factory life—such as 14-hour workdays, hazardous conditions, and child labor—led to immediate demands for change.

    • The Rise of Labor Unions: In response to the social and economic changes brought about by industrial capitalism, many workers in industrialized states organized themselves into labor unions. By banding together to use collective bargaining and strikes, unions successfully fought to:

      • Improve dangerous working conditions.

      • Limit grueling daily work hours.

      • Gain higher wages for the working class.

    • Broadening State Reforms: Beyond the factory floor, various individuals and governments promoted political, social, educational, and urban reforms. These initiatives led to the gradual expansion of franchise (the right to vote), the creation of public education systems, and sanitizing urban layout designs.

    2. Alternative Visions: Socialism and Communism¶

    While unionists worked within the capitalist system to adjust it, deep discontent with established power structures encouraged the development of radical alternative ideologies.

    • The Critique of Capitalism: Intellectuals argued that laissez-faire capitalism inherently exploited the working class (proletariat) to enrich a small group of factory owners (bourgeoisie).

    • Karl Marx and Marxism: German philosopher Karl Marx co-authored The Communist Manifesto, promoting an alternative vision of society that came to be known as socialism and communism.

    • The Communist Vision: Marx argued that history was a series of class struggles. He predicted that the working class would eventually stage a violent revolution, seize the means of production (factories, mines, land), dismantle capitalism, and establish a classless society where wealth was shared equally.

    3. Defensive Modernization in Asia and Africa¶

    Seeing the massive military and economic gap created by Western industrialization, empires in Asia and Africa recognized they needed to adapt. In response, some governments sought to reform and modernize their economies and militaries.

    The Ottoman Empire (The Tanzimat Reforms)¶

    • Known as the "Sick Man of Europe," the Ottoman Empire launched the Tanzimat Reforms (Reorganization) to centralize power and keep pace with Europe.

    • They westernized the legal code, created secular public schools, built modern factories, and declared civic equality for all Ottoman subjects regardless of religion.

    Qing China (The Self-Strengthening Movement)¶

    • Following humiliating defeats by industrialized Western powers in the Opium Wars, China initiated the Self-Strengthening Movement.

    • The goal was to adopt Western military technology and industrial methods (like building modern shipyards and arsenals) while strictly preserving traditional Confucian values and imperial rule.

    Internal Resistance to Modernization¶

    These defensive modernization efforts were not universally embraced. Reform efforts were frequently resisted by some members of the government or established elite groups who feared losing their traditional power and cultural identity:

    • In the Ottoman Empire, conservative religious elites and the Janissaries fiercely opposed westernization.

    • In Qing China, the conservative factions led by Empress Dowager Cixi actively resisted systemic adjustments, keeping the imperial system vulnerable to outside pressure.

    What is Most Important for the Exam?¶

    1. The Spectrum of Responses: Be ready to compare different responses to capitalism. Labor unions represent a moderate, internal reform response, while Karl Marx’s communism represents a radical, systemic overthrow response.

    2. Defensive Reform vs. Elite Resistance: The AP exam loves questions about why the Ottoman Empire and Qing China struggled to industrialize compared to Meiji Japan. The answer is internal resistance from traditional elites. While Japan overthrew the Shogunate to modernize smoothly, conservative elites in China and the Ottoman Empire successfully blocked long-term, systemic reforms.

    3. The Catalyst for Marxism: Remember that Marxism was directly caused by the socioeconomic inequalities of the Industrial Revolution (Topic 5.3/5.4), not the political Enlightenment (Topic 5.1). Marx's ideas were a critique of how industrial machines altered human labor dynamics.
