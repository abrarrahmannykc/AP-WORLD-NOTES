    Unit 7.5 Unresolved Tensions After WW1

    1. The Post-WWI Scene¶

    WW1 didn't end all wars, but paused them. Allies kept talking about global peace, but during the writing of treaties, they chose imperialism over stability.

    2. The Middle East & The Mandate System¶

    During the war, the Allies promised Arab leaders independence if they revolted against the Ottoman Empire. Instead, the Allies made a secret pact behind their backs—the Sykes-Picot Agreement—to divide the territory among themselves.

    • The League of Nations Mandates: Rather than granting independence, the Allies created "Mandates." They claimed these territories in the Middle East and Africa weren't "ready" to govern themselves, so France and Britain had to manage them.

    • The Reality: It was just colonialism with a new, bureaucratic name.

      • France got: Syria and Lebanon.

      • Britain got: Palestine, Transjordan, and Iraq.

    • The Fallout: This betrayal planted the seeds for decades of conflict, border disputes, and deep-seated anti-Western sentiment in the region.

    3. The Betrayal of East Asia: The May Fourth Movement¶

    China fought on the side of the Allies in WWI, sending over 140,000 laborers to the Western Front. Their main goal? To win back the Chinese territories (Shandong Peninsula) that Germany had seized.

    • The Treaty of Versailles Decision: Instead of returning the land to China, the Allies handed the Shandong Peninsula directly over to Japan.

    • The Spark: On May 4, 1919, thousands of angry Chinese students took to the streets of Beijing to protest the Western betrayal and their own government's weakness.

    • The Importance: The May Fourth Movement was a massive turning point. It catalyzed Chinese nationalism, caused intellectuals to reject Western-style democracy, and directly led many (including a young Mao Zedong) to turn toward Soviet Marxism, setting up the birth of the Chinese Communist Party.

    4. Anti-Colonial Resistance Across the Globe¶

    Woodrow Wilson (US President) championed the idea of "Self-Determination"—the concept that ethnic groups should have the right to choose their own government. Colonial subjects took this literally, but the Allies only applied it to white Europeans.

    India: The Rise of Nationalism¶

    • The Broken Promise: India sent over 1 million troops to fight for Britain, expecting self-rule in return. Instead, Britain extended wartime emergency measures that restricted civil liberties.

    • The Massacre: In 1919, British troops fired on an unarmed crowd of peaceful protestors in the Amritsar Massacre, killing hundreds.

    • The Shift: This brutality completely shattered Indian trust in the British Empire. It accelerated the push for full independence, paving the way for Mahatma Gandhi to lead massive non-violent civil disobedience campaigns (like the Salt March).

    Southeast Asia & Africa¶

    • Indochina (Vietnam): A young nationalist named Ho Chi Minh traveled to the Versailles peace conference to plead for Vietnamese independence from France. He was ignored. He eventually turned to Communism to achieve his goals.

    • Pan-Africanism: Intellectuals across Africa and the diaspora organized Pan-African Congresses to demand an end to European colonial rule, organizing local labor strikes and political resistance.
