## Negotiated Independence - India
As India was “Britain’s most valuable colony,” Britain invested a lot of money towards its infrastructure, leading towards modernization.
Although this modernization assisted the British, it helped establish a growing Indian middle class that was influenced by nationalism and self-determination.

**1885** - India forms the Indian National Congress to petition the British government for a greater voice in politics. **Result:** Many petitions were largely ignored.


Even after millions of Indian troops served in WW1, Britain did not entertain hopes of self-determination. Crowds of nonviolent protesters, like ones in the **Amritsar Massacre (1919)** were often met with harsh suppression.
After Mohandas gandhi became leader in **1920**, the resistance movement was then seen as nonviolent.

India demands independence after WW2, and since there were many pro-independence politicians in parliament, Britain recognized an independent India in **1947**. However, Britain also recognized an independent Muslim Pakistan in 1947.
Note: The establishment of India as a new state was met with immense violence.
**1906** - Muslims form the **Muslim League** and called for their own state separate from India.
Muslim and Hindu extremists committed harsh crimes against each other during the Partition of India, and around a million people were killed during this transition.

## Negotiated Independence - Ghana
### Slight Context:
- WW2 Gold Coast Veterans were not happy with colonial conditions
- **1948 Accra Riots** - many political leaders, like **Kwame Nkrumah**, were killed, and protesters were shot 
- **1949** - Nkrumah forms the Convention People’s Party (CPP) and organized protests and strikes via a nonviolent manner.

The Gold Coast Colony and British Togoland granted independence from Britain, and these territories were later renamed ‘Ghana.’

### Nkrumah’s Leadership
- Nkrumah became the first president of Ghana in 1960.
- Nkrumah took inspiration from many nationalist states like the US and created a nation that reflected on its rich past (hence the name change to Ghana), establishing currency, the flag, and museums.
- Nkrumah also initiated many public projects like **hydroelectric plants**.
- Later became corrupt and accused of running the country into debt, establishing a one party state in **1964**.

Nkrumah was a proud supporter of Pan-Africanism, which was the celebration of African ideas across the continent. Eventually, he founded the **Organization of African Unity** in **1963**.
In **1966**, Nkrumah’s government was overtaken by a military coup.

- Ghana eventually created a new constitution in **1992** to have more democratically fair elections.
### Violent Independence - Algeria
- Algerians started to campaign for independence after WW2, which lead to the beginning of the **Algerian War for Independence** in 1954.
- Many French people settled in Algeria, so France decided that Algeria could not break off as a separate colony.
- The National Liberation Front, or FLN, utilized guerrilla techniques against 500 thousand French troops. **Result: hundreds of thousands of Algerias were tortured and/or killed**.
- French President **Charles de Gaulle** eventually planned a referendum for Algeria in which the people of France and Algeria voted, and independence was granted on **1962**.

- Anti-immigration sentiment grew as pro-French Algerians and settlers fled back to France.
- Algerian violence saw between 50K to 150K killed by the FLN and lynch mobs.
- A military coup replaced the first president of the Algerian Republic in **1965**, and the FLN maintained a socialist authoritarian order.
	- On the flip side, agriculture was collectivized and industry was modernized.
- After the Islamic Salvation Front won an election, violence broke out between the FLN and the latter due to a violation of the one-party rule, beginning the **Algerian Civil War (1991-2002)**. The military state of emergency was finally lifted in **2011**.
- Islamist movements challenge Algeria’s growth, as they were keen on using violence to gain popularity. Algeria banned religion-based parties in **1997**.
#### Negotiated Independence in French West Africa
France gave a relatively swift independence to many West African colonies during the **mid-1950s**, this was mostly due to the fact that there were not much French settlers in these regions. 
- France then invested in these regions by inducing agricultural growth and better trade.

## Egypt and the Suez Crisis
- General **Gamal Abdel Nasser** and Muhammad Naguib overthrows the king and replaces it with an Egyptian Republic. 
- Nasser strongly supported Pan-Arabism, the movement that promoted political and cultural unity between Arab nations.
- Nasser employed socialism by turning private farms into systems that implemented irrigation and drainage with the sharing of profit from crops (land reforms).
	- Nasser also nationalized some industries and foreign owned banks, eventually nationalizing the **Suez Canal**.
- **1956** - Nasser seized the Suez Canal, which was administered by both the French and the British, which led to a convert plan between France and Israel to invade Egypt, which eventually involved the UN to resolve the **Suez Crisis**.
- For peacekeeping measures, the US later became involved, and UN peacekeepers were stationed at the sinai peninsula until an agreement was made to open the **Suez Canal** to the world.
We also see that Egypt maintains a “non-aligned stance” between US and USSR here.

## Quebec’s Quiet Revolution
- Quebec was formerly a territory of France, called New France, and it was mostly Catholic compared to British Canada’s Protestantism.
- Quebec became more aligned with France, and in the **1960s, a Quiet Revolution** ensued, in which the Liberal Party started making economic reforms that supported separatist movements.
- Some nationalist groups used terrorism as a means of awareness.
- PM Pierre Trudeau was a native of Quebec, easing tensions.
- **1995** - A referendum to make Quebec independent failed.
