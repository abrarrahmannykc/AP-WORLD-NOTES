
## 1. Neoliberalism and Free-Market Policies

After the Cold War, many Western nations moved toward **Neoliberalism**, an economic approach inspired by _laissez-faire_ capitalism.

- **Key Figures**: Margaret Thatcher (UK) and Ronald Reagan (US).
    
- **Core Ideas**:
    
    - Reduction of government regulation and influence on the economy.
        
    - Tax breaks and financial stimulation for large corporations.
        
    - **"Trickle-Down Economics"**: The belief that financial benefits given to the wealthy and corporations will eventually "trickle down" to the rest of society through job creation and investment.
        

## 2. Agents of Liberalization

Specific leaders pushed for free-market reforms, often through drastic measures:

- **Augusto Pinochet (Chile)**: Led a US-backed coup; implemented "Chicago School" economic policies that privatized assets and opened Chile to global trade.
    
- **Deng Xiaoping (China)**: Though a communist leader, he opened China to the global market via "Socialism with Chinese Characteristics," allowing for Special Economic Zones and foreign investment.
    

## 3. Global Economic Institutions

To stabilize the world economy after WWII and the Cold War, several international organizations were created:

- **World Trade Organization (WTO)**:
    
    - Replaced the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT).
        
    - Aims to facilitate smooth, predictable, and free trade.
        
    - **Criticism**: Critics argue it favors developed nations over developing ones.
        
- **World Bank & International Monetary Fund (IMF)**:
    
    - Provide loans and financial assistance to struggling countries.
        
    - Often require "Structural Adjustment Programs" (austerity measures) in return for aid.
        

## 4. Regional Trade Agreements

Nations formed blocs to eliminate tariffs and trade barriers to boost regional economies:

- **NAFTA (North American Free Trade Agreement)**:
    
    - Created a free-trade zone between the US, Canada, and Mexico.
        
    - Later replaced by the **USMCA**.
        
    - **Controversy**: While it increased trade, some argue it led to the loss of manufacturing jobs in the US and exploited cheaper labor in Mexico.
        
- **Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN)**: Promotes economic growth and regional stability in Southeast Asia.
    

## 5. Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

MNCs are businesses that operate in multiple countries, wielding immense economic and political power.

- **Examples**: Nestlé, Nissan, Mahindra & Mahindra.
    
- **Case Study: Nestlé**:
    
    - **Pros**: Invests in sustainable agricultural methods and trains farmers.
        
    - **Cons**: Has faced heavy criticism for using child labor in cocoa production and for commodifying water (treating it as a product rather than a human right).
        

## 6. Knowledge Economies

In the late 20th century, the global economy shifted toward "Knowledge Economies," where growth is driven by technical and scientific innovation.

- **Key Sectors**: IT, pharmaceuticals, and education.
    
- **Geographic Shift**: Manufacturing moved to developing nations (like Vietnam or Bangladesh), while developed nations focused on design, software development, and services.
