# AP World History Unit 1 Comprehensive Review: The Global Tapestry (c. 1200 to c. 1450)

Unit 1 explores the major states and civilizations across the globe during the post-classical era. The core focus is on how states organized themselves, maintained stability, and utilized belief systems to secure political legitimacy.

---

## 1. East Asia: The Song Dynasty & The Confucian Bureaucracy

The Song Dynasty (960–1279) stood as the wealthiest and most population-dense empire of the 13th century, relying on deeply centralized administrative traditions.

### State Structure and Bureaucracy

- **The Imperial Bureaucracy**: The Song government utilized a massive administrative system to carry out imperial policies. To join the bureaucracy, candidates had to pass rigorous **Civil Service Examinations**.
    
- **Meritocracy & The Scholar Gentry**: The exam system allowed for a degree of upward social mobility, shifting power away from traditional military aristocrats to a highly educated class of civilian officials known as the scholar gentry.
    
- **The Mandate of Heaven**: The emperor's political legitimacy was tied to this concept, asserting that emperors ruled by divine favor but could lose it if they grew corrupt or failed to protect the populace.
    

### Cultural and Religious Foundations

- **Filial Piety**: The duty of family members to subordinate their needs and desires to those of the male head of the family or the ruler. This formed the bedrock of Song social stability.
    
- **Neo-Confucianism**: A syncretic philosophical system that emerged as a response to Buddhism and Daoism. It infused traditional Confucian ethical practices with metaphysical abstract thought, emphasizing social hierarchy and intellectual cultivation.
    
- **Spread of Buddhism**: Buddhism traveled from India along trade networks. Key syncretic branches emerged:
    
    - _Theravada_: Emphasized monastic life and strict meditation.
        
    - _Mahayana_: Transformed Buddhism into a popular religion with spiritual deities (_Bodhisattvas_), making it highly accessible across China, Korea, and Japan.
        
    - _Zen/Chan_: Blended Buddhist meditation with native Daoist principles.
        

### Economic and Technological Expansion

- **Champa Rice**: A fast-ripening, drought-resistant strain of rice gifted to China from Vietnam. It allowed for two harvests per year, triggering an unprecedented population explosion.
    
- **Grand Canal Expansion**: The state expanded this massive internal waterway network, linking the agricultural south with the political north, creating the world's most vibrant domestic market.
    
- **Proto-Industrialization**: Rural peasants shifted toward mass manufacturing silk, porcelain, and iron tools in home workshops for commercial sale.
    
- **Innovations**: The invention of woodblock printing lowered the cost of books, boosting literacy. Navigational tools like the magnetic compass and the production of gunpowder altered global warfare and trade.

### New Political Entities

- **The Seljuk Empire**: Turkic nomadic warriors from Central Asia advanced into the Middle East, reducing the Abbasid Caliph to a religious figurehead while holding real military-political power under the title of _Sultan_.
    
- **The Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt**: Mamluks were enslaved Turkic soldiers purchased by Arab rulers. They launched a successful coup, establishing a prosperous state that controlled key trade lanes through Egypt and the Levant.
    
- **The Delhi Sultanate**: Turkic-Afghan forces conquered northern South Asia, establishing an Islamic state in a heavily Hindu region.
    

### Cultural and Intellectual Transfers

Dar al-Islam acted as a crucial engine for global knowledge, preserving ancient Mediterranean texts and making major breakthrough discoveries:

- **The House of Wisdom (Baghdad)**: A state-funded academic research hub where scholars translated Greek, Persian, and Indian mathematical and philosophical manuscripts into Arabic.
    
- **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi**: A brilliant polymath who made critical advancements in astronomy, medicine, and mathematics, laying the groundwork for modern trigonometry.
    
- **Sufism**: A mystical branch of Islam focused on personal, introspective connection with God. Sufi missionaries played an essential role in spreading Islam into Sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia because they willingly adapted Islamic ideas to fit local traditions.

## 3. South and Southeast Asia: Religious and Regional States

South and Southeast Asia remained politically decentralized, relying on a mix of Hinduism and Buddhism to validate political power.

### South Asian State Dynamics

- **The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646)**: Established in Southern India by two brothers who converted from Islam back to Hinduism. It stood as a powerful Hindu state that resisted northern military incursions.
    
- **The Bhakti Movement**: A popular Hindu reform movement emphasizing intense, emotional devotion to a single personal deity (like Krishna or Shiva) rather than strict Vedic rituals. Similar to Sufism, it challenged traditional caste boundaries, making it highly attractive to lower-caste populations.
    

### Southeast Asian Maritime and Land Empires

- **Srivijaya Empire (650–1377)**: A powerful maritime empire based on the island of Sumatra. It controlled the strategic **Strait of Malacca**, growing wealthy by taxing the international shipping lanes moving between India and China.
    
- **Majapahit Kingdom (1293–1527)**: A sea-based Buddhist empire centered on Java that successfully managed regional luxury spice trade networks.
    
- **Khmer Empire / Angkor (802–1431)**: A land-based agrarian empire in modern Cambodia. Its architectural evolution showcases cultural shifts: the capital began as a massive Hindu monument (Angkor Wat) dedicated to Vishnu and was later structurally adapted into a Buddhist temple complex.

## 4. The Americas: Imperial Innovation in Isolation

Lacking access to Afro-Eurasian trade networks, complex civilizations in the Americas built innovative agricultural and administrative systems to manage diverse ecological zones.

### Mesoamerica: The Aztec (Mexica) Empire

- **The Tribute System**: The Aztecs ruled indirectly over conquered city-states through terror and economic extraction. Subject populations were forced to regularly deliver textiles, luxury feathers, military supplies, and sacrificial victims to the capital, Tenochtitlan.
    
- **Chinampas**: Innovative agricultural technology consisting of artificial floating gardens built on shallow lake beds, boosting crop yields to feed a dense urban population.
    
- **Religious Legitimacy**: The state weaponized public human sacrifice rituals, claiming it was spiritually necessary to prevent the sun from dying and to show off political supremacy.
    

### The Andes: The Inca Empire

- **Bureaucratic Centralization**: Unlike the Aztecs, the Inca built a deeply centralized state. They divided their vast territory into four distinct administrative provinces overseen by royal governors.
    
- **The Mita System**: A mandatory, state-managed public labor service required of all citizens. Households built expansive road networks, bridges, and mountain agricultural terraces as a form of tax payment to the state.
    
- **The Quipu**: Lacking a formal written alphabet, the Inca used a system of knotted colored strings to record complex mathematical data, census tracking, and economic inventories.

## 5. Africa: Decentralized States and Trade Integration

African states during this era developed along distinct paths, shaped by their geography and integration into global trade networks.

- **The Mali Empire (West Africa)**: Integrated into the **Trans-Saharan trade route**, exporting gold and slaves in exchange for Saharan salt.
    
    - _Mansa Musa_: His famous pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324 demonstrated Mali's enormous gold wealth to the Mediterranean world. He transformed Timbuktu into a center for Islamic scholarship and book production.
        
- **The Swahili Coast (East Africa)**: A network of independent, commercial city-states (like Kilwa and Mombasa) linked by the **Indian Ocean trade**. Merchants blended native Bantu cultures with Arabic Islamic traditions, creating the distinct Swahili language and identity.
    
- **Great Zimbabwe**: A powerful, inland South African kingdom that controlled the flow of gold from interior mines to coastal Swahili ports. It is famous for its massive, mortarless stone architecture (_The Great Enclosure_).
    
- **Ethiopia**: An isolated Christian kingdom in East Africa. Surrounded by expanding Islamic states, it preserved its religious identity, carving distinct rock-hewn churches directly into the volcanic soil.

## 6. Europe: Feudal Fragmentation and the Catholic Thread

Following the collapse of the Western Roman Empire, Europe was politically decentralized, fragmented into small, local territories, and defined by a rigid agrarian hierarchy.

### The Feudal Hierarchy

- **Political Feudalism**: A decentralized mutual-defense system based on land-for-loyalty agreements:
    
    - _Monarchs_ granted land (_fiefs_) to noble _Lords_.
        
    - _Lords_ hired _Knights_ (vassals) to act as military protection.
        
- **Economic Manorialism**: Small, self-sufficient economic manors where peasants and **Serfs** lived. Serfs were legally bound to the land they worked, providing agricultural labor and crops to the lord in exchange for physical safety.
    

### The Power of the Catholic Church

- **The Universal Institutional Thread**: Because Europe lacked a unified king or emperor, the Roman Catholic Church functioned as the primary transnational political authority.
    
- **The Great Schism (1054)**: The Christian church formally split into the Roman Catholic Church in Western Europe and the Eastern Orthodox Church in the Byzantine Empire (Eastern Europe).
    
- **The Crusades**: Pope Urban II called on Christian knights to launch holy wars to reclaim Jerusalem from Islamic control. While militarily unsuccessful, the Crusades reconnected Europe to Arab trade and science networks, increasing the demand for Asian luxury goods.
