
# AP World History Mega-Review Note: Major Ideologies (c. 1750 to 1900)

The long 19th century was defined by an explosion of political, economic, and social **ideologies**. These systems of ideas developed either to justify the massive changes brought about by revolutions and industrialization, or to challenge established power structures and fight for marginalized groups.

---

## 💰 1. Economic Ideologies: Capitalism vs. Socialism

The rise of the mechanized factory system sparked a fierce debate over how wealth should be generated, managed, and distributed.

### Laissez-Faire Capitalism

- **Core Thinker**: **Adam Smith** (authored _The Wealth of Nations_, 1776).
    
- **The Philosophy**: Governments should abandon the restrictive regulations of mercantilism and adopt a hands-off (**laissez-faire**) approach to the economy. Smith argued that markets are self-regulating and driven by an **"invisible hand"**—the natural forces of supply, demand, and individual self-interest.
    
- **Key Features**:
    
    - Protection of **private property rights**.
        
    - Free market competition with minimal state tariffs or intervention.
        
    - The belief that global trade and consumer self-interest automatically increase a nation's total wealth.
        

### Socialism & Marxism (Communism)

- **Core Thinkers**: **Karl Marx** and **Friedrich Engels** (authored _The Communist Manifesto_, 1848).
    
- **The Philosophy**: A direct radical response to the harsh exploitation, low wages, and dangerous conditions of early industrial capitalism. Marx argued that all human history is defined by a continuous **class struggle** between those who own the factories and land (the **bourgeoisie**) and those who must sell their labor to survive (the **proletariat**).
    
- **Key Features**:
    
    - Belief that capitalism inherently exploits the working class to enrich the minority elite.
        
    - Prediction that the proletariat would eventually launch a violent revolution to overthrow the bourgeoisie.
        
    - The ultimate goal: abolishing private property and placing the **means of production** (factories, mines, tools) under collective public ownership to create a classless, stateless communist society.

## 🏛️ 2. Political Ideologies: Liberalism, Conservatism, & Nationalism

The political chaos of the Atlantic Revolutions split thinkers into opposing camps regarding the speed of social reform and the true source of government legitimacy.

### Classical Liberalism

- **Core Thinkers**: John Locke, John Stuart Mill, Voltaire.
    
- **The Philosophy**: Rooted deeply in the **Enlightenment**. It prioritized individual liberties, human intellect, and the legal limitation of state and religious power over citizens.
    
- **Key Features**:
    
    - Advocacy for **natural rights** (life, liberty, property) protected by written constitutions.
        
    - Support for representative republican governments, free speech, freedom of religion, and equality under the law.
        
    - _Note on Context_: 19th-century classical liberals generally favored limiting voting rights to wealthy, educated, land-owning men, fearing that total democracy would lead to mob rule.
        

### Conservatism

- **Core Thinker**: Edmund Burke.
    
- **The Philosophy**: Developed as a direct, reactionary backlash against the radical violence of the French Revolution (such as the Reign of Terror). It argued that human society is fragile and should rely on time-tested traditions rather than sudden, rationalist overhauls.
    
- **Key Features**:
    
    - Defense of established traditional authorities, including **monarchies, organized religion, and social aristocracies**.
        
    - A belief that gradual organic change is far safer than sudden revolutionary upheavals.
        

### Nationalism

- **The Philosophy**: The revolutionary idea that a group of people who share a common language, history, culture, and geographic territory form a distinct **nation**, and that this nation has a sovereign right to govern itself independently.
    
- **The Dual Nature of Nationalism**:
    
    - **Unification**: Used to merge fragmented regional states into massive, cohesive powers (e.g., the complex political unifications of **Germany** and **Italy** in the late 1800s).
        
    - **Disruption**: Used by ethnic minorities to destabilize and break away from sprawling, multi-ethnic land empires (e.g., Greek and Balkan nationalisms fracturing the declining Ottoman Empire).

## ✊ 3. Social Reform Ideologies: Feminism & Abolitionism

Enlightenment assertions that all humans possess universal natural rights naturally encouraged marginalized groups to challenge systemic social inequalities.

### Emergent Feminism

- **Core Thinkers**: Mary Wollstonecraft (_A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_), Olympe de Gouges (_Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen_).
    
- **The Philosophy**: The systematic demand for political, educational, and economic equality for women, actively exposing the hypocrisy of male revolutionary leaders who preached "universal liberty" while denying basic civic rights to women.
    
- **Key Milestone**: **The Seneca Falls Convention (1848)**. Organized in New York by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott, activists drafted the _Declaration of Sentiments_ (modeled directly on the U.S. Declaration of Independence) to formally demand women's suffrage—the right to vote.
    

### Abolitionism

- **The Philosophy**: The international movement to completely end the transatlantic slave trade and abolish chattel slavery.
    
- **The Catalysts**: Fueled by a combination of Enlightenment principles regarding natural liberty, religious moral awakenings (like Quaker and evangelical movements), and the historic radical success of the **Haitian Revolution**.
    
- **The Outcome**: Led to the legal ban of the slave trade by Britain and the US in the early 1800s, followed by the gradual statutory emancipation of enslaved populations across the Atlantic world over the course of the 19th century.

## 🌍 4. Imperialist Ideologies: Social Darwinism & Cultural Superiority

As industrialized Western nations built massive global empires in Africa and Asia during the 19th century, they developed pseudoscience and racial theories to morally justify their violent territorial conquests.

### Social Darwinism

- **Core Thinker**: Herbert Spencer.
    
- **The Philosophy**: A pseudoscientific misapplication of Charles biological theory of evolution ("survival of the fittest") to human societies, races, and nations.
    
- **The Justification**: Imperialists argued that white Europeans were biologically evolved to be superior, wealthy, and powerful, while non-white populations were "less evolved." Therefore, European conquest, displacement, and economic exploitation of foreign cultures was viewed as a natural, unavoidable law of human progress.
    

### The Civilizing Mission & "The White Man's Burden"

- **The Philosophy**: The paternalistic cultural belief that Europeans had a moral obligation and duty to conquer non-Western peoples in order to "civilize" them.
    
- **The Justification**: Imperial conquest was framed not as an aggressive resource grab, but as a selfless, charitable burden. Europeans argued they were bringing the benefits of Western civilization—including Christianity, modern medicine, capitalism, technologies like railroads, and European education—to societies they condescendingly viewed as backward or ignorant.

## 💡 Top Core Themes to Remember for the AP Exam

1. **Ideologies Reflect the Material World**: Notice the direct cause-and-effect loops. Enlightenment political overreach caused **Liberalism and Conservatism**; factory floor exploitation caused **Socialism and Marxism**; and imperial resource extraction caused **Social Darwinism**.
    
2. **The Multi-Use Tool of Enlightenment**: Enlightenment ideas were a double-edged sword. Revolutionary elites used them to break free from kings; women and abolitionists used them to demand social equality; and later, colonized peoples used those exact same Western ideals of liberty to justify fighting back against European empires.
    
3. **Pseudoscience as a Shield**: Always remember that concepts like Social Darwinism were explicitly constructed after the fact to act as an intellectual shield—it allowed industrial nations to reap the vast economic rewards of global imperialism while claiming they were doing a moral service for humanity.
