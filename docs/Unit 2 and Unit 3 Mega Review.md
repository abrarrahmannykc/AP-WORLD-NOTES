
# AP World History Unit 2 Comprehensive Review: Networks of Exchange (c. 1200 to c. 1450)

Unit 2 focuses on the expansion of interregional trade networks that bound Afro-Eurasia together. The core elements to track are the technologies that made trade possible, the rise of the Mongol Empire, and the cultural and environmental ripple effects of global connectivity.

---

## 1. The Silk Roads

The Silk Roads connected East Asia to the Mediterranean world. During this era, trade shifted from small-scale luxury bartering to high-volume commercial networks.

### Causes of Expansion

- **Imperial Stability**: The expansion of powerful states—such as the Song Dynasty in China and the Abbasid Caliphate in the Middle East—provided political security, standardized currencies, and protected infrastructure for long-distance merchants.
    
- **Commercial Innovations**:
    
    - **Caravanserai**: Fortified roadside inns spaced roughly one day's travel apart along the Silk Roads. They provided food, water, and shelter for merchants and their pack animals, acting as vital commercial nodes and centers for cultural exchange.
        
    - **The Flying Cash System**: The Song Dynasty developed a credit system where merchants could deposit paper money in the capital and redeem it for cash in distant provinces, eliminating the need to carry heavy copper coins.
        
    - **Banking Houses & Bills of Exchange**: European and Islamic merchants adopted written bills of exchange (agreements to pay a set amount of money at a future date), facilitating legal, long-distance lines of credit.
        

### Effects of Expansion

- **Demand for Luxury Goods**: High-volume trade spurred proto-industrial production in China and Persia. Artisans scaled up the manufacturing of silk, porcelain, iron goods, and textiles.
    
- **Urbanization**: Trade routes birthed vibrant, cosmopolitan trading cities. Nodes like **Samarkand** (in modern Uzbekistan) and **Kashgar** (in western China) thrived because they sat at the crossroads of major desert routes, boasting massive markets and fresh water supplies.
    

---

## 2. The Mongol Empire and the Making of Afro-Eurasia

Temüjin unified the fragmented pastoral nomadic tribes of the Mongolian steppe, taking the title **Genghis Khan** in 1006. He initiated the largest contiguous land empire in human history.

### Conquest Tactics and Governance

- **Military Organization**: The Mongol military relied on absolute meritocracy, unmatched equestrian archery, psychological warfare, and the rapid adoption of foreign siege technology (such as Chinese catapults).
    
- **Incorporation of Conquered Peoples**: Rather than wiping out local cultures, the Mongols co-opted local elites and bureaucrats to manage their complex empire. For example, they utilized literate Persian scribes to run regional tax administrations.
    
- **The Pax Mongolica**: A period of relative peace and stability across Eurasia during the 13th and 14th centuries. The Mongol state unified the entire length of the Silk Roads under a single legal code (the _Yassa_), standardizing passports, protecting merchants, and sending trade volume to its historical peak.
    

### Fragmentation into Khanates

Following the death of Genghis Khan, the empire fractured into four distinct political realms, each adapting to local cultural traditions:

1. **The Yuan Dynasty (China)**: Established by Kublai Khan. He claimed the Mandate of Heaven, rebuilt the capital at Khanbaliq (Beijing), but barred ethnic Chinese from holding top government offices and abolished the civil service exams.
    
2. **The Ilkhanate (Middle East)**: Located in Persia. The Mongol rulers heavily assimilated, eventually converting to Islam and patronizing Islamic science and architecture.
    
3. **The Chagatai Khanate**: Maintained traditional nomadic lifestyles in the heart of Central Asia.
    
4. **The Golden Horde (Russia)**: Ruled Russia indirectly from the steppes. They forced fragmented Russian principalities (like the Grand Duchy of Moscow) to pay heavy silver tribute, inadvertently elevating Moscow's political dominance over other Russian cities.
    

---

## 3. Indian Ocean Maritime Trade

The Indian Ocean functioned as the world's premier arena for mass consumer trade, connecting East Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and Southeast Asia.

### Environmental Mastery and Technology

Maritime merchants did not sail at random; they had to master the **Monsoon Winds**. Winds blew predictably northeast in the summer and southwest in the winter, forcing merchants to plan their travels around seasonal weather cycles.

- **Maritime Inventions**:
    
    - **The Dhow Ship & Lateen Sail**: Arab and Indian sailors utilized triangular lateen sails to catch the wind from any direction, allowing ships to tack against the wind.
        
    - **The Junk Ship**: Massive Chinese transport vessels featuring internal watertight compartments, making them highly stable and capable of carrying tons of heavy commercial cargo.
        
    - **The Astrolabe and Compass**: Perfected by Islamic and Chinese scholars, these tools allowed sailors to accurately calculate latitude and maintain directional courses in the open ocean.
        

### Key Trading Nodes

- **The Sultanate of Malacca**: A strategic Islamic state in Southeast Asia that grew wealthy by building a navy to patrol the **Strait of Malacca**. They taxed every commercial merchant vessel moving between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea.
    
- **Diasporic Communities**: Because monsoon winds forced merchants to stay in foreign ports for months at a time, traders permanently settled in coastal cities. Arab and Persian merchants in India, and Chinese merchants in Southeast Asia, introduced their native religions, languages, and cultures into local societies.
    

---

## 4. Trans-Saharan Trade Networks

The massive sands of the Sahara Desert were bridged by trading networks that integrated West Africa into the broader Mediterranean and Islamic economies.

- **The Camel Saddle**: The invention of specialized pack-saddles allowed North African Berbers to travel across the desert securely. Camels could walk for days without water, turning the hostile Sahara into an active commercial highway.
    
- **The Gold-Salt Trade**: West African empires (like Mali) sat on massive, rich gold mines. They traded their gold and enslaved labor to North African merchants in exchange for life-saving Saharan rock salt, luxury textiles, and horses.
    
- **Mansa Musa's Global Impact**: Mali's wealthiest ruler traveled to Mecca in 1324 with an immense caravan of gold. He distributed so much gold in Cairo that he caused massive inflation and depressed the regional value of gold for over a decade, proving West Africa's economic power to the Mediterranean world.
    

---

## 5. Cultural and Environmental Connectivity

The acceleration of trade networks caused ideas, technologies, ecosystems, and pathogens to travel faster than ever before.

### Cultural Innovations and Travelers

- **Religious Syncretism**: Religions adapted as they traveled. Zen Buddhism blended Indian ideas with Chinese Daoism, while Neo-Confucianism spread from China to shape Korean and Japanese state policies. In Africa and Southeast Asia, Islam blended with local animist and ancestral traditions.
    
- **Intellectual Transfers**: Papermaking and gunpowder technology traveled west from China via the Mongol Empire, fundamentally altering European printing, literacy, and warfare.
    
- **The Major Travelers**:
    
    - **Marco Polo**: An Italian merchant who traveled the Silk Roads to serve in the court of Kublai Khan. His book introduced a skeptical Europe to China's immense wealth and urbanization.
        
    - **Ibn Battuta**: An Islamic scholar who traveled over 73,000 miles across Dar al-Islam (Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and Southeast Asia). His journals offer an unparalleled look at how safely and widely Islam bound the Afro-Eurasian world together.
        

### Environmental Consequences

- **Agricultural Diffusions**: High-calorie crops spread to new regions. **Champa Rice** expanded throughout China, while items like **bananas** (introduced to Africa from Southeast Asia) and **citrus crops** altered diets, cleared landscapes, and fueled population booms.
    
- **The Black Death (Bubonic Plague)**: Originating in Central Asia, the flea-borne pathogen traveled rapidly along trade routes opened by the Pax Mongolica.
    
    - _The Toll_: Between 1346 and 1353, the plague wiped out 30% to 60% of Europe's population and significant percentages of Chinese and Middle Eastern populations.
        
    - _The Social Shock_: The catastrophic loss of labor fractured the feudal system in Western Europe. Surviving peasants demanded higher wages and lower rents, breaking the traditional bond of serfdom.

# AP World History Unit 3 Comprehensive Review: Land-Based Empires (c. 1450 to c. 1750)

Unit 3 explores the consolidation and centralization of powerful land-based empires across Afro-Eurasia. These states—often called "Gunpowder Empires"—rebuilt their administrations, expanded their territories through firearm military tech, and weaponized culture, art, and religion to maintain political legitimacy.

---

## 1. Imperial Expansion: The Power of Gunpowder

The post-1450 era saw the decline of decentralized feudal systems and the rise of massive, land-based states that relied heavily on gunpowder artillery, cannons, and muskets to conquer expansive territories.

### The Islamic Gunpowder Empires

- **The Ottoman Empire**: Centered in Anatolia. In 1453, **Sultan Mehmed II** used massive gunpowder cannons to breach the triple walls of Constantinople, destroying the Byzantine Empire and renaming the city **Istanbul**. The empire expanded across North Africa, the Middle East, and into Southeastern Europe.
    
- **The Safavid Empire**: Established in Persia by Shah Ismail. The Safavids utilized military force to declare **Twelver Shia Islam** the mandatory state religion, directly challenging their Sunni Muslim neighbors.
    
- **The Mughal Empire**: Founded by Babur (a descendant of Genghis Khan). Mughal forces marched into northern South Asia, using advanced artillery to establish a wealthy Islamic state over a vast, defensive Hindu majority population.
    

### East Asian and Eurasian Expansion

- **The Qing Dynasty (Manchus)**: Hailing from Manchuria, the Manchus marched south, overthrew the corrupt Ming Dynasty in 1644, and established the Qing Dynasty. They expanded China's borders into Central Asia, conquering Tibet, Mongolia, and Xinjiang through intensive military campaigns.
    
- **The Russian Empire**: Emerging from the Grand Duchy of Moscow, Czars like **Ivan IV (The Terrible)** used gunpowder forces to push east across the Ural Mountains into Siberia, absorbing nomadic lands and building a transcontinental fur-trading empire.
    

---

## 2. Imperial Administration: Centralizing State Power

To manage massive, diverse populations, empires replaced decentralized local rulers with highly loyal, professional administrative systems and state-managed militaries.

### Recruitment and Bureaucratic Systems

- **The Ottoman Devshirme System**: The Ottoman state forcibly recruited young Christian boys from the Balkan provinces. They were converted to Islam, educated in state administration, and trained to enter either the elite imperial bureaucracy or the **Janissaries**—a highly disciplined, professional standing army loyal exclusively to the Sultan.
    
- **The Mughal Zamindar System**: The Mughal emperors utilized local aristocrats called _zamindars_ to act as regional tax collectors. They collected agricultural revenues from the peasantry and delivered a set percentage to the central treasury, ensuring state funding.
    
- **The Russian Boyars & Peter the Great**: Peter the Great centralized Russian power by stripping the traditional noble class (the _boyars_) of independent authority. He forced them into compulsory state service and military roles, compelling them to westernize their dress, grooming, and manners.
    

### Consolidating Power in East Asia

- **The Tokugawa Shogunate (Japan)**: Following centuries of civil war, Tokugawa Ieyasu unified Japan in 1600. To control the autonomous regional warlords (**Daimyo**), the Shogun enforced the **Alternate Attendance System**. Daimyo were forced to maintain lavish residences in the capital city of Edo (Tokyo) and live there every other year, effectively keeping them under state surveillance and draining their finances.
    
- **Qing Civil Service and Segregation**: The Manchus retained the traditional Confucian Civil Service Exams to keep ethnic Han scholars cooperative. However, they maintained strict cultural separation: they banned intermarriage between Manchus and Han Chinese and forced Han men to wear the **queue** hairstyle (shaved forehead with a long braid) as a public display of submission to Manchu rule.
    

---

## 3. Legitimizing Rule: Art, Architecture, and Divine Religion

Rulers did not rely solely on military force; they built massive public monuments and leveraged religious beliefs to prove their divine right to govern.

### Monumental Architecture and Royal Art

Empires spent enormous sums on visual displays of wealth to project power and intimidate foreign rivals:

- **The Palace of Versailles (France)**: King Louis XIV built a massive, baroque palace complex outside Paris. He forced his nobles to live there with him, transforming potential political rebels into competing courtiers distracted by courtly etiquette.
    
- **The Taj Mahal (Mughal India)**: Built by Emperor Shah Jahan as a mausoleum for his favorite wife. The structure blended traditional Islamic geometry, Persian arches, and Indian white marble, acting as a visual testament to Mughal wealth.
    
- **Imperial Art**: Qing Emperors commissioned grand imperial portraits displaying themselves surrounded by books and Confucian artifacts to prove their literacy and legitimacy to their Chinese subjects. Ottoman Sultans commissioned elaborate miniature paintings detailing their military triumphs and courtly ceremonies.

### Religious Ideology and State Fracture

- **The Protestant Reformation (1517)**: German monk Martin Luther pinned his _95 Theses_ to a church door, protesting Catholic corruption (like the sale of indulgences). Armed with the printing press, his ideas spread rapidly, fracturing Christian Europe into Catholic and Protestant states and triggering devastating conflicts like the **Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)**.
    
- **The Safavid-Ottoman Conflict**: This geopolitical war was fueled by deep religious division. The Sunni Ottoman Empire and the Shia Safavid Empire engaged in centuries of border warfare, locked in ideological conflict over who was the legitimate leader of the Islamic world.
    
- **Divine Right vs. Mandate of Heaven**: European monarchs claimed the **Divine Right of Kings**, asserting they were God's direct representatives on Earth and answerable only to Him. In contrast, East Asian empires relied on the _Mandate of Heaven_, which required the ruler to govern morally or risk losing power through natural disasters or rebellions.

Armed with the printing press, his ideas spread rapidly, fracturing Christian Europe into Catholic and Protestant states and triggering devastating conflicts like the **Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)**.
    
- **The Safavid-Ottoman Conflict**: This geopolitical war was fueled by deep religious division. The Sunni Ottoman Empire and the Shia Safavid Empire engaged in centuries of border warfare, locked in ideological conflict over who was the legitimate leader of the Islamic world.
    
- **Divine Right vs. Mandate of Heaven**: European monarchs claimed the **Divine Right of Kings**, asserting they were God's direct representatives on Earth and answerable only to Him. In contrast, East Asian empires relied on the _Mandate of Heaven_, which required the ruler to govern morally or risk losing power through natural disasters or rebellions.

## 💡 Key Conceptual Understandings for the Exam

1. **The Double-Edged Sword of Trade**: Notice the clean handoff between Unit 2 and Unit 3. The immense wealth generated by the trade networks of Unit 2 (Silk Roads, Indian Ocean) provided the tax revenues land-based monarchs used to buy the expensive gunpowder and professional armies needed to build the empires of Unit 3.
    
2. **Bureaucracy over Aristocracy**: To centralize control, successful empires took power away from traditional local nobles (like European feudal lords, Russian boyars, or Japanese daimyo) and handed it to professional bureaucrats loyal only to the head of state.
    
3. **The Weaponization of Religion**: Religion was rarely just a matter of personal faith for these empires; it was a core tool of statecraft. Rulers used religious orthodoxy to enforce conformity, justify external expansion, and validate their political authority.
