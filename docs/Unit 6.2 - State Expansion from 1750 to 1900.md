## 2. The Scramble for Africa

Before 1880, European presence in Africa was mostly limited to coastal trading ports. By 1914, Europeans controlled roughly 90% of the continent.

###  The Berlin Conference (1884–1885)

- **The Goal**: To avoid a catastrophic pan-European war over territory, German Chancellor Otto von Bismarck called Western nations together to carve up Africa orderly.
    
- **The Catch**: **Zero African leaders were invited** or consulted.
    
- **The Impact**: Europeans drew artificial borders based entirely on geometric lines and resource locations. They deliberately lumped rival ethnic groups together and split cohesive kingdoms apart, creating deep-seated tribal and national instability that persists today.
    

### The Congo Free State

- **The Owner**: King Leopold II of Belgium claimed the Congo basin as his personal, private corporate property rather than a colony of the Belgian state.
    
- **The Commodity**: **Rubber**. The global industrial demand for bicycle and car tires made rubber extraction immensely lucrative.
    
- **The Brutality**: Total state terror. Indigenous laborers who missed their strict rubber collection quotas had their hands chopped off by state militias, and mass executions were rampant. Following a global humanitarian outcry, the Belgian parliament stepped in and stripped Leopold of his private ownership, converting it into the _Belgian Congo_ in 1908.
    

###  South Africa & Egypt

- **The Suez Canal (1869)**: Built by a French company using Egyptian labor, this canal connected the Mediterranean to the Red Sea, cutting travel time to Asia in half. Britain eventually bought up Egypt's debt, seizing fiscal and military control of the country to secure this vital imperial passage.
    
- **The Boer War (1899–1902)**: In South Africa, the British clashed heavily with the **Boers** (descendants of early Dutch settlers) over control of rich gold and diamond mines. Britain won, consolidating South Africa into its global empire.

## 3. Imperialism in South and Southeast Asia

### British India: From Company to Crown

- **The Shift**: Originally, British presence was managed entirely by a joint-stock company, the **British East India Company (EIC)**, utilizing its own private corporate military.
    
- **The Turning Point**: The **Sepoy Rebellion (1857)**. Indian soldiers (Sepoys) in the EIC army revolted over rumors that their rifle cartridges were greased with cow and pig fat (violating Hindu and Muslim religious laws).
    
- **The Result**: The rebellion was brutally crushed, the EIC was dissolved, and the British government took direct control of the subcontinent. This period of direct British state governance is known as the **British Raj** (1858–1947).
    

### The Southeast Asian Scramble

- **The Dutch East Indies (Indonesia)**: The Dutch government took over from the bankrupt Dutch East India Company (VOC), forcing locals to grow cash crops (spices, coffee, rubber) for export rather than subsistence food crops.
    
- **French Indochina**: France conquered modern-day Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia, prioritizing rubber plantations and mineral extraction.
    
- **The Exception: Siam (Thailand)**: King Chulalongkorn skillfully modernized Siam's economy, military, and infrastructure while playing British and French diplomatic rivalries against each other. Siam successfully maintained its independence as a neutral buffer state.

## 4. East Asia: Imperialism via Economic Strangling

Unlike Africa or India, Western powers generally did not establish direct colonies in East Asia; instead, they used economic warfare to carve up declining land-based empires.

### China's Spheres of Influence

- **The Problem**: The Qing Dynasty was failing to industrialize or modernize.
    
- **The Tactic**: Following Western victories in conflicts like the **Opium Wars**, European powers and Japan forced China to sign "Unequal Treaties."
    
- **Spheres of Influence**: Rather than conquering land, countries like Britain, France, Germany, Russia, and Japan carved out economic zones. Within its designated sphere, a foreign country held exclusive trade and mining rights.
    

### The Rise of Imperial Japan

- **The Shift**: Facing Western intrusion (Commodore Perry's arrival), Japan chose to industrialize at lightning speed during the **Meiji Restoration (1868)**.
    
- **The Student Becomes the Master**: Lacking domestic resources, Japan mimicked Western imperialism:
    
    - **First Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895)**: Japan defeated China, gaining control of Taiwan and dominance over Korea.
        
    - **Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905)**: Japan shocked the world by destroying the Russian Baltic Fleet, establishing itself as the undisputed imperial superpower of East Asia.
