
# Non-Violent Resistance

### Mohandas Gandhi:
- developed a philosophy of nonviolent resistance called Satyagraha (meaning devotion to truth/civil disobedience).
- **Salt March (1930)** - Led a 240-mile march to the sea to protest the British monopoly on salt.
- **Homespun Movement** - Encouraged Indians to spin their own cloth to boycott British textiles.
India eventually gained independence in 1947.

### Martin Luther King Jr.
- A prominent leader of the American **Civil Rights Movement**.
- **Montgomery Bus Boycott** - Led a 381-day boycott of the city's bus system after Rosa Parks was arrested.
- **March on Washington (1963)** - Delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech to advocate for an end to racism and for better civil rights.
America eventually passed the **Civil Rights Act of 1964** and the **Voting Rights Act of 1965**.

### Nelson Mandela

#### Definition:
Apartheid - a policy or system of segregation or discrimination on grounds of race.

- Originally a non-violent protestor with the African National Congress, but later sabotaged government property after the state starting enacting violence on peaceful protestors.
- He eventually spent 27 years in prison.
- After his release in 1990, he negotiated the end of Apartheid and became South Africa's first black president in 1994.

## Challenges to Soviet Power in Eastern Europe
- **(1956) Poland** - workers demonstrated against Soviets and demanded better living conditions. **Result:** The collectivization of farms ended and a new secretary of the Polish Communist Party, Wladyslaw Gomulka, pursued independent domestic policy in Poland.
- **(1956) Hungary** - Leader Imre Nagy declared freedom from Soviet control and demanded Soviets troops to evacuate. He was a supporter for free elections and advocated for neutrality. **Result:** Soviet invasion and execution.

### 1968: The Year of Revolt
#### Czechoslovakia (Prague Spring of 1968):
- Leader Alexander Dubcek introduced "Socialism with a human face" that included freedoms of speech and press.
- Fearing the spread of democracy, the Soviet Union and Warsaw Pact allies invaded Czechoslovakia in August 1968.
- **The Brezhnev Doctrine:** Following this event, the USSR established a policy stating that the Soviet Union had the right to intervene in any Eastern Bloc country where communism was under threat.

### Terrorism as an Act of Resistance:
- **Northern Ireland:** All of Ireland except Northern Ireland gained independence from the UK in 1922. There was tension between Catholics (that supported independence) and Protestants that refused. Around 3,500 died in their conflict, and some members of the Irish Republican Army (Catholic supported) used terrorism to achieve political ends (like setting off bombs in London).
- **Spain:** The Basque region in Spain wanted independence and formed the Basque Homeland and Freedom organization. The ETA killed more than 800 people, and killed dictator Francisco Franco in 1973. It declared an end to violent actions in 2011.
- **Peru**: Former philosophy professor Abimael Guzman built a Mao-inspired organization called **Shining Path** that performed many assassinations to overthrow the existing government. Around 37K people were killed and continued attacks into the late 90s.
- **(Extremist) Islamic Terrorism**:
	- They had a fundamentalist interpretation of Islam and most Muslims condemned them.
	- Most victims were Muslims, and many cities like Madrid, London, and Paris were attacked.
	- Examples: The Taliban in Afghanistan, Al-Qaeda's attack during 9/11

### Response of Militarized States to Internal Conflicts:
##### Francisco Franco's Spain:
- **The Conflict:** Ethnic minorities (like Basques and Catalans) and political dissidents (socialists, communists, and liberals) challenged his centralized, traditionalist rule.
- **The Response:** Franco used his military and secret police to execute or imprison hundreds of thousands of "enemies of the state." He banned regional languages and used the military to enforce strict cultural and political conformity.

##### Uganda's Idi Amin:
- **The Conflict:** Internal opposition from different ethnic groups and those loyal to the previous president (Milton Obote).
- **The Response:** Known as the "Butcher of Uganda," Amin used "State Research Bureau" (military death squads) to purge his own army and civilian population. He also expelled the entire Asian (Indian and Pakistani) minority population in 1972, seizing their businesses to give to his military supporters.

##### Brazil (Military Dictatorship 1964-1985):
In 1964, a US-backed military coup overthrew the Brazilian government to prevent what they feared was a communist lean.
- **The Conflict:** Student activists, labor unions, and urban guerrillas protested the military's suspension of constitutional rights.
- **The Response:** The military government passed **Institutional Act No. 5**, which suspended _habeas corpus_, legalized torture of political prisoners, and censored all media. The military controlled the judiciary and used "disappearances" to silence critics.
