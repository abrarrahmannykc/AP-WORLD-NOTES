
# <u>Israel</u>
**1890s** - Zionism movement born as a response to the Dreyfus Affair (French Jewish officer Alfred Dreyfus was falsely accused of giving information to Germany). Theodor Herzl formed the Zionist movement.

Zionist demand a state in Palestine as Jews historically lived in that region in the past, but Palestine was already inhabited and was mostly Arab Muslims.

**1917** - **Balfour Declaration**: Arthur Balfour writes a letter to Lord Rothschild, where Britain promises a national home for the Jewish people while promising Arab leaders an independent state in the region.
**1918** - The collapse of the Ottoman Empire allowed European Jews to be brought to Palestine, and Britain subsequently gave the Israel-Palestine administration to the hands of the UN.

**1948** - The UN divides Palestine into Jewish and Arab sections, thus forming Israel.

### Wars:
#### 1956: Response to the Suez Crisis
Israel, backed by France and Great Britain, invades the Sinai Peninsula in part to liberate the Suez Canal.

#### 1967: Six Day War:
Israel gains Egypt’s Gaza Strip, the West Bank, Jordan’s East Jerusalem, and Syria’s Golan Heights.

#### 1973: Yom-Kippur War
Israel successfully defeats Egypt and Syria against their surprise invasion.

The Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO) rejects the **Camp David Accords** (which offered peace between Israel and Egypt) due to the desire to the return of occupied lands. The PLO was lead by Yasser Arafat.

## <u>Cambodia</u>
### Context:
- After Japan's surrender in WW2, France took back the colony of French Indochina (which included Cambodia).
- To gain international support for Cambodian independence, King Sihanouk goes into "self-imposed exile" in 1953 and tours Western nations like the US.
- Cambodia formally became independent on November 1953. It tried to maintain a non-aligned status, but was pressured by the North Vietnamese army to join the Vietnam War to use its land as supply routes.
- In 1970, Sihanouk was overthrown by pro-American General Lon Nol.

Eventually, a communist guerrilla organization called the **Khmer Rouge** took power (leader: Pol Pot).
It's form of communism was ruthless and implemented a system similar to China's cultural revolution, targeting intellectuals.

#### Definition:
Brain drain - the emigration of highly trained or intelligence people from a particular country

Cambodia had one of the severest instances of brain drain in world history. It was left with almost no doctors, teachers, or experts. As a result, Cambodia became one of the poorest nations in the world.

**2 Million Deaths - 1/4th of Cambodia's pop.**

**1977** - Vietnam invades Cambodia to stop Pol Pot's Regime and the Khmer Rouge. **Result**: Vietnamese Victory. Vietnam helped Cambodia reach some internal stability and completely withdrew all forces in 1989.

Prince Sihanouk returned to Cambodia as a constitutional monarch in 1993 and Cambodia started to implement democratic policies.

## <u>Tanzania</u>
- Tanzania gained independence from Britain in 1961.
- Julius Nyerere became the first president of the United Republic of Tanzania and emphasized self-reliance and traditional African values.

**1967 Arusha Declaration** - a document of Tanzania's political and economic identity.
Key Points:
- self-reliance -> the country would mostly rely on **land and people** and not foreign aid to avoid neo-colonialism.
- The elimination of class divisions (part of Ujamaa, Swahili for "familyhood").
- Development should be measured by the well-being of people (ex. education and health), rather than by GDP.
- Forbade officials from owning shares in companies -> eliminating the potential of a ruling elite.

**Successes:**
- Implemented Swahili as a **national language**, unifying over 120 ethnic groups.
- Tanzania achieved one of the **highest literacy rates** in Africa.
- Access to **clean water** and rural clinics increased significantly, so life expectancy also increased.

**Failures:**
- The forced collectivization of farms was often resisted and was highly inefficient.
- By the late 1970's, self-reliance dropped and Tanzania became heavily dependent on foreign aid.
- State corporations were often mismanaged.

Nyerere resigned the presidency in 1985.

#### Definition:
Metropole - a large city of a former colonial ruler

Examples:
London - people from India, Pakistan, East Africa, and the Caribbean moved to London for better opportunities and work.
Paris - After the Algerian War and the decolonization of West Africa (Senegal, Mali, Ivory Coast), large numbers of migrants moved to Paris.
Amsterdam - Significant migration occurred from former Dutch East Indies territories and Suriname.
- Impact -> Dutch cuisine has Indonesian and Surinamese influences.
