
# AP World History: Comparing Unit 5 (Revolutions) & Unit 6 (Consequences of Industrialization)

To master the AP World History exam, you must understand that **Unit 5 and Unit 6 are not separate eras; they are happening at the exact same time (1750–1900)**.

|**Feature**|**Unit 5: Revolutions**|**Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization**|
|---|---|---|
|**Core Focus**|**Internal Transformation:** How the West changed politically (Enlightenment) and economically (Factories).|**External Projection:** How industrialized nations expanded outward to build empires.|
|**Economic Driving Force**|The invention of the factory system, steam engine, and free-market capitalism.|The desperate need for raw materials (rubber, oil, cotton) and new consumer markets.|
|**Global Power Dynamics**|Western Europe, the US, and Meiji Japan industrialize while Asian/Middle Eastern shares decline.|Industrialized nations conquer or economically dominate Africa, Asia, and Latin America.|
|**Human Migration**|**Rural-to-Urban:** Peasants move from farms to crowded industrial cities.|**Global/Transoceanic:** Workers move across oceans for labor (indentured servants, diasporas).|

##  Key Intersections: How Unit 5 Drives Unit 6

### 1. Technology: Production (5.5) vs. Conquest (6.4)

- **In Unit 5**, nations mastered the steam engine, built steel factories, and laid domestic railroads to speed up factory production.
    
- **In Unit 6**, those exact technologies were weaponized. Western powers put steam engines on warships (gunboats) and used steel to build advanced weapons, allowing them to force open Chinese ports (Opium Wars) and carve up Africa.
    

### 2. Economics: Industrial Capitalism (5.7) vs. Economic Imperialism (6.5)

- **In Unit 5**, businesses evolved into transnational corporations (like Unilever or HSBC) and embraced _laissez-faire_ free markets to maximize factory efficiency.
    
- **In Unit 6**, these corporations outgrew their home countries. Unilever pushed into West Africa and the Belgian Congo to violently extract palm oil for factory machinery; HSBC managed the wealth generated from selling opium to China.
    

### 3. Human Responses: Class Protests (5.8) vs. Anti-Colonial Resistance (6.3)

- **In Unit 5**, the victims of capitalism were the domestic factory workers. They responded by forming **labor unions** or advocating for **Marxist communism** to fix things at home.
    
- **In Unit 6**, the victims of capitalism were the colonized peoples abroad. They responded with **anti-imperial resistance movements**, such as the 1857 Rebellion in India, the Yaa Asantewaa War in West Africa, or religious rebellions like the Ghost Dance in the U.S..
    

### 4. Migration: Domestic Urbanization (5.9) vs. Global Diasporas (6.6 & 6.7)

- **In Unit 5**, migration was mostly internal. Enclosure acts and agricultural changes forced peasants off farms and into polluted industrial cities.
    
- **In Unit 6**, migration became global. Imperialism disrupted local economies so severely that millions of Chinese, Indian, and Irish laborers migrated across oceans to work on imperial plantations, mines, and railroads, creating ethnic enclaves (like Chinatowns) globally.
