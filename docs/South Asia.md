## 🗺️ Unit 7: Global Conflict (1900–1945)

### Unit 7.3 - Conducting World War I: The Imperial Lifeline

- **The Imperial Burden**: Great Britain could not have sustained its war effort without its crown jewel colony, British India. The colony was subjected to **Total War mobilization** on an industrial scale.
    
- **Manpower**: India sent over **1.3 million soldiers and laborers** to serve in the British Army. They fought across the Western Front in Europe, East Africa, and extensively throughout the Middle East against the Ottoman Empire.
    
- **The Colonial Bargain**: The British government explicitly hinted at moving toward self-governing status or dominion status for India to secure the cooperation of the Indian National Congress (INC).
    
- **Economic Strain**: India's domestic economy was heavily rationed. Raw materials, textiles, and grain were systematically seized and shipped out to support British forces on the home and war fronts, creating steep inflation and local shortages.
    

### Unit 7.5 - Unresolved Tensions After WWI: The Broken Promises

- **The Rowlatt Acts (1919)**: Instead of granting autonomy after WWI, Britain extended wartime emergency measures. These laws allowed the colonial government to imprison political activists without trial and heavily censor the press.
    
- **The Amritsar Massacre (April 13, 1919) 🩸**:
    
    - **The Event**: Thousands of unarmed, peaceful Indian demonstrators gathered in Jallianwala Bagh (a walled public garden) in Amritsar to protest the Rowlatt Acts and celebrate a local festival.
        
    - **The Action**: British Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer blocked the only exit and ordered his troops to fire continuously into the trapped crowd until they ran out of ammunition.
        
    - **The Toll**: Officially, 379 were killed, but independent estimates placed the death toll well over 1,000.
        
    - **The Turning Point**: This brutality shattered any lingering illusions of benevolent British rule. It radicalized moderate independence advocates, pushed **Mahatma Gandhi** to the forefront of the nationalist movement, and catalyzed the _Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922)_.
        

### Unit 7.7 - Conducting World War II: The Fractured Colony

- **Forced Entry**: In 1939, the British Viceroy declared India at war with Germany without consulting a single elected Indian politician. This triggered widespread outrage and mass resignations within the Indian National Congress.
    
- **The War Effort**: Despite internal political chaos, India mobilized the largest volunteer army in human history during WWII—over **2.5 million soldiers**. They fought in North Africa, Italy, and played a crucial role in stopping Japanese expansion in Burma.
    
- **The Fractured Response**:
    
    - **The Nationalist Track**: Gandhi and the INC launched the **"Quit India" Movement (1942)**, refusing to back the war effort unless immediate independence was granted. The British responded by jailing the entire top tier of leadership for the duration of the war.
        
    - **The Militant/Collaborative Track**: Subhas Chandra Bose split from the pacifist mainstream and formed the **Indian National Army (INA)**. Backed by Imperial Japan, the INA fought against British forces in Southeast Asia, attempting to liberate India by military force.
        
- **The Bengal Famine (1943) 🌾**: Driven by wartime scorched-earth policies, the cutoff of rice imports from Japanese-occupied Burma, and prioritizing food distribution for Allied military forces over locals, an estimated **2 to 3 million people starved to death** in Bengal.

## 🏛️ Unit 8: Cold War and Decolonization (1945–1900s)

### Unit 8.2 - The Cold War: Geopolitical Alignment & Strategy

- **The Strategic Buffer**: Because of its vital location bordering the Indian Ocean, Central Asia, and China, South Asia became a critical diplomatic playground for both the US and the USSR.
    
- **The Proxy Dynamic**: While India leaned toward a socialist economic structure and independent foreign policy, neighboring Pakistan formally aligned with Western anti-communist blocs (joining SEATO and CENTO) to secure American military aid against India.
    

### Unit 8.4 - Spread of Communism After 1900: Regional Land Reform

- **The Naxalite Insurgency**: Inspired by Mao Zedong’s successful peasant mobilization in China, radical communist factions (the Naxalites) launched a protracted, violent guerrilla war starting in the late 1960s in rural West Bengal. They targeted wealthy landlords to forcibly redistribute land to impoverished peasants and indigenous tribal groups.
    

### Unit 8.5 - Decolonization after 1900: Negotiated Independence

- **The Financial Collapse of Empire**: Exhausted, bankrupted, and broken by the end of World War II, Great Britain could no longer afford the administrative or military cost of occupying a deeply rebellious India.
    
- **Negotiated Shift**: Independence was achieved through diplomatic negotiations between the British state, Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru representing the secular **Indian National Congress**, and Muhammad Ali Jinnah representing the **Muslim League**.
    

### Unit 8.6 - Newly Independent States: The Partition Matrix

- **The Two-Nation Theory**: Muhammad Ali Jinnah argued that Hindus and Muslims comprised two distinct, irreconcilable nations, demanding a separate homeland for Muslims to prevent systemic marginalization in a Hindu-majority India.
    
- **The Divide (August 1947)**: The subcontinent was partitioned into two sovereign nations:
    
    - **The Dominion of India**: Secular, Hindu-majority.
        
    - **The Dominion of Pakistan**: Islamic state, geographically split into West Pakistan and East Pakistan (separated by 1,000 miles of Indian territory).
        
- **The Cataclysm**: The rapid drawing of the **Radcliffe Line** borders triggered the largest mass migration in human history.
    
    - **The Chaos**: Roughly **15 million people** panicked and fled across newly created borders to find safety within their respective religious majorities.
        
    - **The Violence**: Sectarian riots, trains targeted for massacres, and systemic violence resulted in the deaths of **500,000 to 2 million people**.
        
- **The Kashmir Conflict**: The princely state of Jammu and Kashmir had a Muslim majority but a Hindu ruler. The ruler signed his territory over to India, sparking the first Indo-Pakistani War in 1947. Kashmir remains a highly militarized, disputed border zone to this day.
    
- **The Birth of Bangladesh (1971)**: East Pakistan faced systematic economic exploitation and political neglect from the West Pakistan ruling elite. In 1971, East Pakistan launched a war of independence, backed by Indian military intervention, and successfully seceded to become the independent nation of **Bangladesh**.
    

### Unit 8.7 - Global Resistance to Power Structures: Non-Alignment

- **The Bandung Conference (1955)**: India's first Prime Minister, **Jawaharlal Nehru**, emerged as a principal architect of the **Non-Aligned Movement (NAM)**.
    
- **The Ideology**: NAM sought to navigate a middle path through the dangerous bipolar world of the Cold War. Nehru refused to align India formally with either the United States or the Soviet Union.
    
- **The Practical Benefit**: By remaining neutral, India successfully secured financial and developmental assistance from both superpowers—receiving Soviet aid for heavy state industries (like steel mills) and American agricultural technology.

## 🌐 Unit 9: Globalization (1900–Present)

### Unit 9.2 - Technological Advances and Limitations: Disease

- **Malaria & Cholera**: As a tropical region historically lacking robust water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) infrastructure in rural areas, South Asia struggled heavily with poverty-related illnesses.
    
- **Eradication Campaigns**: India launched aggressive, state-managed vaccination and sanitation programs, successfully eliminating smallpox and polio, while drastically reducing malaria mortality through international partnerships with groups like the World Health Organization (WHO).
    

### Unit 9.3 - Environmental Impacts in the Global Age: The Green Revolution

- **The Looming Crisis**: By the mid-1960s, India’s booming population outpaced its traditional agrarian food supply, leaving the nation highly vulnerable to catastrophic famines.
    
- **The Intervention**: Scientists introduced high-yielding varieties (HYV) of wheat and rice, advanced irrigation infrastructures, and massive inputs of chemical fertilizers and pesticides.
    
- **The Results**:
    
    - **The Good**: India transitioned from a starving nation reliant on foreign food aid to a self-sufficient, top global food exporter.
        
    - **The Bad**: Massive environmental degradation, depletion of water tables in states like Punjab, chemical runoff into drinking water, and high financial costs that squeezed out small, poor farmers.
        

### Unit 9.4 - Economics in the Global Age: The Knowledge Economy Shift

- **The Liberalization of 1991**: Facing a severe balance-of-payments crisis, India moved away from its old, highly regulated socialist command model (often called the "License Raj") and opened its economy to global markets.
    
- **The Tech Explosion**: India heavily prioritized STEM education, producing a massive, fluent English-speaking workforce.
    
- **The Knowledge Economy**: Cities like **Bengaluru (Bangalore)** transformed into premier global tech hubs. Major multinational corporations outsourced their software engineering, IT infrastructure support, and business processing operations directly to India, establishing it as a pillar of the globalized 21st-century service economy.
    

### Unit 9.5 - Calls for Reform and Responses: Social Progress

- **Caste Discrimination Reform**: The 1950 Indian Constitution, drafted under the guidance of Dalit leader **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar**, formally outlawed untouchability and discrimination based on caste.
    
- **Affirmative Action**: The state implemented a robust quota system (Reservations) in government jobs and universities for Dalits and lower-caste groups to correct centuries of systemic inequality, though social tensions surrounding the caste system persist.
