
# AP World History Unit 5 Review Notes: Economic Developments and Innovations (Topic 5.7)

Topic 5.7 covers the dramatic shifts in economic systems, ideologies, and financial structures that emerged to support and expand global industrial capitalism between 1750 and 1900.

---

##  1. The Death of Mercantilism & Rise of Free Markets

As industrial factories began churning out goods at an unprecedented volume, old economic models proved too restrictive to handle the sheer scale of wealth creation.

- **Abandoning Mercantilism**: Western European countries began abandoning mercantilism—the old system focused on state-controlled trade, heavy tariffs, and hoarding gold bullion.
    
- **Embracing Adam Smith**: Instead, nations adopted free trade policies in response to the growing acceptance of Adam Smith’s theories of **laissez-faire capitalism**. Smith argued that market forces (the "invisible hand") should drive economic production, free from heavy-handed government intervention.
    

---

##  2. Transnational Businesses & New Financial Systems

Because building transcontinental railroads, deep-sea shipping fleets, and massive industrial factories required massive pools of money, the ways businesses organized completely transformed.

- **Transnational Businesses**: The global nature of trade and production led to the rise of massive transnational businesses that relied on new, sophisticated banking and finance practices. These companies operated across national borders to extract resources and secure consumer markets.
    

### Key Illustrative Examples of Transnational Corporations

- **Hong Kong and Shanghai Banking Corporation (HSBC)**: Established by British merchants in 1865 to finance the booming, highly lucrative trade between Europe and China following the Opium Wars.
    
- **Unilever**: Based jointly in England and the Netherlands, this massive consumer goods company operated extensively across British West Africa and the Belgian Congo to secure massive supplies of palm oil for soap production.
    

### Innovations in Financial Instruments

To minimize the immense financial dangers of global trade, new financial instruments were popularized:

- **Stock Markets**: Allowed individuals to buy and sell partial ownership shares of major companies, dispersing investment risks and pooling massive corporate funding.
    
- **Limited-Liability Corporations (LLCs)**: A revolutionary legal development ensuring that if a corporation went bankrupt or was sued, individual investors only lost the money they had directly put into the company, protecting their personal fortunes.
    

---

## 3. The Impact of Industrial Capitalism on Daily Life

The development of widespread industrial capitalism thoroughly reshaped global society:

- **Increased Standards of Living**: For some social classes (particularly the newly wealthy bourgeoisie and the growing professional middle class), industrial capitalism brought a notable increase in the overall standard of living.
    
- **The Rise of Consumer Culture**: Continued improvements in manufacturing methods steadily drove down production costs, drastically increasing the raw availability, affordability, and overall variety of consumer goods on open markets. Shopping transformed from a necessity into a leisure activity.
    

---

## What is Most Important for the Exam?

1. **Ideology Shift (Laissez-Faire)**: Be ready to explain the cause-and-effect loop. The shift from _mercantilism_ to _laissez-faire capitalism_ directly caused the explosion of private factory ownership and global trade networks.
    
2. **The Purpose of Transnational Corporations**: On an essay or SAQ, use **HSBC** or **Unilever** to demonstrate how industrialized Western nations economically dominated non-industrialized regions (like China or West Africa) without needing to directly colonize them politically.
    
3. **Reducing Risk**: Understand that **Stock Markets** and **Limited Liability** were structural innovations designed to reduce individual financial risk. Without these protections, everyday investors would have been too terrified to fund high-risk global infrastructure projects like transoceanic telegraph cables or cross-continental railways.
