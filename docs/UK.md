
# AP World History Mega-Review Note: The Evolution of Great Britain (c. 1200 to 1900)

Tracking Great Britain (England) across the AP World History curriculum requires understanding how an island nation on the periphery of Afro-Eurasia transformed a fractured feudal system into a pioneering constitutional monarchy, built the world's first industrial economy, and weaponized that wealth to construct history's largest transoceanic maritime empire.

---

## 🏰 Unit 1 & 2: Feudalism, Global Isolation, and the Wool Trade (c. 1200–1450)

During the post-classical era, England was a decentralized, second-tier agrarian kingdom structurally far less wealthy or population-dense than Song China or the Abbasid Caliphate.

- **Political Feudalism & Magna Carta**: England relied on a decentralized system where land-owning lords provided military knights to the king. In **1215**, rebellious nobles forced King John to sign the **Magna Carta**, a landmark document limiting the absolute power of the monarch and establishing the principle that the king must respect the rule of law. This laid the early structural framework for the later creation of a representative **Parliament**.
    
- **Economic Manorialism**: Everyday life was structured around self-sufficient manors where peasant serfs were legally tied to the soil.
    
- **The Black Death Shift**: When the Bubonic Plague arrived in 1348 via maritime trade routes, it wiped out nearly half the population. The catastrophic loss of labor completely fractured the manorial system. Surviving peasants demanded wages, breaking the traditional cycle of serfdom far earlier than in Eastern Europe or Russia.
    
- **The Wool Connection**: England's early economy thrived by raising sheep and exporting raw wool across the English Channel to textile weavers in Flanders, forming its first major commercial relationship with the broader European market.

## 🧭 Unit 3 & 4: Constitutional Consolidation & Maritime Empire (c. 1450–1750)

The early modern era saw England systematically reject political absolutism, build strong protections for private merchant property, and violently enter global maritime trade networks.

### 1. Rejection of Absolute Monarchy

While continental European states (like France under Louis XIV) successfully built absolute monarchies, England followed a completely different political path:

- **The English Civil War (1642–1651)**: A bloody internal war between King Charles I (who claimed the absolute _Divine Right of Kings_) and Parliament (led by wealthy puritan merchants). Parliament won and executed the king.
    
- **The Glorious Revolution (1688)**: Parliament bloodlessly overthrew King James II and placed William and Mary on the throne.
    
- **The English Bill of Rights (1689)**: To take the crown, the new monarchs signed a legal agreement permanently establishing a **Constitutional Monarchy**. Power was shared with Parliament, individual civil liberties were protected, and arbitrary royal tax overreach was made illegal. This political stability and legal protection of private property gave English merchants unparalleled confidence to invest capital into long-distance trade ventures.
    

### 2. Building the Maritime Trading Post Empire

Lacking the land-based massive armies of the Ottomans or Ming Dynasty, England looked directly to ocean channels to expand its global influence.

- **Mercantilism**: England adopted an economic model designed to maximize state wealth by exporting more than it imported, heavying tariffs on foreign goods, and exploiting overseas colonies for raw materials.
    
- **Joint-Stock Companies**: To pool the massive financial resources needed for high-risk transoceanic voyages, the state granted charters to private joint-stock ventures. The crown jewel was the **British East India Company (EIC)**, founded in 1600. The EIC functioned as a highly armed corporate state: it raised private mercenary armies, built fortified coastal trading posts in India (Bombay, Calcutta, Madras), and aggressively displaced Portuguese and Dutch spice networks.
    
- **The Triangular Trade and Plantation Complex**: In the Atlantic region, Great Britain became a dominant operator in the transatlantic triangular slave trade network. British manufacturing firms shipped firearms and textiles to West Africa, transported enslaved human cargo via the brutal **Middle Passage** to the Americas, and returned raw plantation commodities (tobacco, sugar, cotton) back to Liverpool and London.

## 🏭 Unit 5: The Cradle of the Industrial Revolution (c. 1750–1900)

Great Britain was the distinct geographic cradle of the Industrial Revolution, fundamentally transforming humanity's relationship with production and the environment.

### 1. The Ingredients for Industrialization

Britain possessed a unique confluence of environmental, social, and economic advantages that allowed it to industrialize before any other nation:

- **The Coal and Iron Layout**: Britain's landscape sat directly on top of massive, easily accessible deposits of coal (to fuel machines) and iron ore (to build machinery and infrastructure).
    
- **Waterway Accessibility**: The island's dense network of navigable rivers and deep-sea ports allowed for the ultra-cheap, rapid internal transport of industrial components.
    
- **The Enclosure Acts & Urbanization**: Parliament passed laws allowing wealthy landowners to buy up and enclose traditional public farming lands. This forced millions of small-scale, displaced rural peasants off farms and directly into growing cities, creating a concentrated, cheap labor pool for factories.
    
- **Imperial Captive Markets**: The massive maritime colonies built in Unit 4 provided Britain with an unlimited supply of raw inputs (like American and Indian raw cotton) and a captive global market to sell its factory-produced goods.
    

### 2. The Tech Shift: The Factory System

- **Steam and Textiles**: James Watt’s refinement of the **steam engine** allowed factories to abandon their dependence on fast-moving rivers for watermills. Factories could now be built inside cities near massive pools of urban labor, powered by burning fossil fuels like coal. The textile industry was the first to fully mechanize using spinning jennys and power looms.
    
- **Specialization of Labor**: Production shifted from skilled artisans crafting a single item at home (the cottage industry) to centralized factories where workers performed repetitive, single steps along an assembly line.
    
- **The Global Manufacturing Trap**: As cheap, British machine-made fabrics flooded world markets, they undercut and crushed traditional handmade textile manufacturing hubs in Asia and North Africa, drastically reducing the global manufacturing share of India and Egypt.
    

### 3. Social Realignments & Domestic Backlash

- **The Class Split**: Industrialization shattered old social hierarchies, creating a wealthy manufacturing **Bourgeoisie**, a professional **Middle Class** (managers, accountants), and a vast, impoverished **Industrial Working Class** (proletariat).
    
- **The Gender Separation**: In working-class families, women and children worked out of sheer economic necessity in dangerous textile mills for low wages. In middle-class families, women were excluded from the public business world, bound instead by the **"Cult of Domesticity"** which idealized the home as a moral, non-commercial environment focused purely on childcare and domestic duties.
    
- **The Reform Backlash**: Horrific urban pollution, housing shortages, and child labor prompted immense backlash. Workers formed **Labor Unions** to utilize strikes and collective bargaining for shorter hours and safer conditions, while intellectuals like Karl Marx used the bleak conditions of British industrial towns to write his critique of capitalism, predicting a violent socialist revolution.

## 🌍 Unit 6: The Imperial Juggernaut & Economic Imperialism (c. 1750–1900)

By the 19th century, Great Britain used its industrial wealth, steel-hulled steamships, and precision weaponry to transition from loose trading posts into direct territorial conquest and financial domination over weaker nations.

### 1. The Shift to Direct State Control

- **The British Raj (India)**: Following the Indian Rebellion of 1857, the British government officially dissolved the private East India Company, stripping it of authority and bringing the entire subcontinent under direct, formal British crown governance (the Raj). India was dubbed the "Jewel in the Crown" of the empire, serving as a vital source of raw cotton and a captive market for British manufactured exports.
    
- **The Scramble for Africa**: At the 1884 Berlin Conference, Britain aggressively claimed massive swathes of Africa, pursuing a "Cape-to-Cairo" continuous line of territory stretching from South Africa to Egypt to control diamonds, gold, and agricultural commodities.
    

### 2. Economic Imperialism

In regions where Britain did not want the administrative cost of setting up an official colony, it used asymmetric trade relationships, debt leverage, and gunboat diplomacy to exploit foreign markets.

- **The Opium Wars (China)**: To correct a massive trade deficit caused by European obsession with Chinese tea, the British East India Company began smuggling illegal Indian opium into Qing China. When Chinese officials seized and destroyed the opium shipments, Britain deployed advanced ironclad steam warships to easily shatter China's coastal defenses.
    
- **The Treaty of Nanking (1842)**: Britain forced China to sign a deeply unequal treaty, opening Chinese ports to foreign merchants, handing over complete territorial control of Hong Kong, and carving out exclusionary **Spheres of Influence**.
    
- **Financial Dominance in Argentina**: British capitalists invested billions of dollars to build Argentina’s modern railway network, harbor ports, and cold-storage meat processing infrastructure. Without ever setting up an official political colony, British firms completely dominated Argentina's export profits, turning its national economy into a subordinate supplier of raw beef to London.

## 💡 Top Core Themes to Remember for Great Britain

1. **Constitutional Stability over Absolutism**: Britain’s early rejection of absolute monarchy created a uniquely stable legal environment that rigorously protected private property rights, giving its merchant class a major structural head start in global trade and capitalism.
    
2. **Private Capital-Driven Industrialization**: Unlike Russia or Meiji Japan, where modernization was forced top-down by the central state, the Industrial Revolution in Great Britain was fundamentally powered from the bottom-up by private inventors, commercial bank loans, and free-market capitalism.
    
3. **The Production-Conquest Feedback Loop**: Britain's history is the ultimate evidence of how Unit 5 and Unit 6 feed into one another. Factories back home required raw resources; this drove industrial armies to conquer foreign lands; those conquered lands provided the resources to build even more advanced technology to conquer the next region.
