
# AP World History Unit 5 Review Notes: Industrialization & Government’s Role (Topic 5.6)

Topic 5.6 looks at how different governments responded to the rise of Western industrial power. While some states embraced state-sponsored industrial projects to protect their autonomy, others faced internal reforms or expanded their influence to keep pace globally.

---

## 1. State-Sponsored Visions of Industrialization

As the influence of the Industrial Revolution grew globally, a small number of states outside of Western Europe realized they had to modernize rapidly or risk being conquered or dominated. These states promoted their own state-sponsored visions of industrialization:

### Egypt under Muhammad Ali

- **The Context**: Following the chaos of Napoleon’s invasion of Egypt, an Ottoman officer named **Muhammad Ali** seized power.
    
- **State Control**: To break free from Ottoman control and prevent European dominance, Ali bypassed the traditional elite and took state control of Egypt's economy.
    
- **Industrial Initiatives**: He heavily backed the development of a state-sponsored **cotton textile industry** and modernized the military by building domestic armaments factories and shipyards.
    

### Japan during the Meiji Era

- **The Context**: For over two centuries, the Tokugawa Shogunate kept Japan isolated. In 1853, U.S. Commodore Matthew Perry arrived with steam-powered warships, forcing Japan to sign unequal trade treaties.
    
- **The Meiji Restoration (1868)**: Fearing Western colonization, a group of reformers overthrew the Shogunate and restored power to the Emperor (Meiji), sparking a massive wave of internal reform.
    
- **State-Driven Modernization**: The expansion of U.S. and European influence in Asia led directly to these internal reforms in Japan. The Meiji government:
    
    - Built state-owned railroads, telegraph systems, and factories, later selling them to wealthy family-owned conglomerates called **Zaibatsu**.
        
    - Abolished the traditional samurai class and established a modern, conscripted military based on Western models.
        
- **The Result**: These targeted, state-supported reforms quickly transformed Japan into a prominent, growing regional power capable of competing on the global stage.

##  2. Western Consolidation and Expansion

While countries like Egypt and Japan built up their domestic industries defensively, established Western powers used their industrial wealth to press their economic and political advantage elsewhere:

- **Expanding Influence in Asia**: Industrialized Western nations used their advanced technology (like steamships and precision weaponry) to force open Asian markets.
    
- **The Contrast in Strategies**: While the West grew its economic advantage through private enterprise and corporate networks, rising states like Meiji Japan counterbalanced this pressure by directly coordinating economic development from the capital.
    

---

##  What is Most Important for the Exam?

1. **The Core Definition of "State-Sponsored"**: Unlike Great Britain, where industrialization was largely driven by private businessmen and capital markets, industrialization in Egypt and Japan was directly funded, planned, and managed by the central government.
    
2. **Perry as a Catalyst for Meiji Japan**: Expect questions that ask you to explain _why_ Japan changed so fast. The expansion of U.S. and European economic networks across Asia was the direct external pressure that forced Japan to abandon isolation and quickly adopt Western industrial systems.
    
3. **Comparing Ali and the Meiji Reforms**: Both Muhammad Ali and the Meiji Emperor used industrialization as a shield against European imperialism. Be ready to use them as primary examples for any essay question regarding state responses to global industrialization from 1750 to 1900.
