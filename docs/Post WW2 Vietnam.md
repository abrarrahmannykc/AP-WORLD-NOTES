
## Historical Context:
- France transformed the Vietnamese economy into a colonial system.
	- Focused on exporting rubber, coal, and rice.
- Land was concentrated in the hands of a few elites, creating class divisions and groups of peasants **without lands.**
- France built railways and ports to facilitate resource extraction.
- Indigenous people excluded from their own government.
- Birth of nationalist movements -> eventually communist movements led by the working class.


## Unit 8.4 - Spread of Communism After 1900
*This demonstrates land reforms in Vietnam.*

Although Japan occupied Vietnam during WW2, France still claimed it as a colony. Vietnam then declared independence in 1945, both from France and Japan.
As mentioned before, only a few elites owned land, and communists sought to seize this land and redistribute it to the peasants, leading to peasant support in (mostly) Northern Vietnam. Communists sometimes violently enforced their policies, while in South Vietnam, the government was slow to implement land reform. **Result:** The South Vietnamese government became growingly unpopular.

## Unit 8.5 - Decolonization After 1900
*This demonstrates a major example of decolonization through armed struggle, also known as a violent independence.*

**Ho Chi Minh** arises as the communist leader of North Vietnam, appealing to nationalist ideals of uniting Vietnam. France then responds to this sentiment with armed forces, leading to a Vietnamese war of independence (1946-1954). Vietnam was then independent, but split into a communist North and capitalist South.

**Ngo Dinh Diem**, on the other hand, was a pro-Western, anti-Communist ruler of South Vietnam, leading as president from 1955-1963. He rose to power via a rigged referendum against Emperor Bao Dan in 1955.
- Diem failed to implement land reforms, which increased support for Viet Cong.

**1956** - A planned election between Ngo Dinh Diem and Ho Chi Minh that aimed to reunite the country.
- **Problem**: Many South Vietnamese people (backed by the United States) opposed communist rule. Diem refused to participate, as he expected Ho Chi Minh to win by a landslide. Thus no election took place.

### Vietnam War (1955-1975):
*In the context of decolonization, many North Vietnamese people and people in the Viet Cong saw the U.S. as the final phase of the anti-colonial revolution, for they viewed the U.S. backed south as a “puppet state.”*

#### Definition - Viet Cong
Southern Vietnamese people that supported the Communists in the North.

#### Definition - Guerrilla War
An irregular form of warfare involving small, mobile groups through ambushes and hit-and-run attacks to fight a traditionally large army.

The fight between the Communist North (backed by Viet Cong troops) and South Vietnam (backed by US troops) is a prime example of guerrilla warfare.
American casualties kept piling up, and people became more vocal about anti-war in America.

**1971** - Richard Nixon begins to withdraw U.S. troops
**1975** - All U.S. troops have been withdrawn, and the Vietnam War concludes after the fall of Saigon, the former capital of South Vietnam.

##### Consequences:
- 1 to 2 million people died, including 50K+ Americans.
- Communism spread to Laos and Cambodia.
- Vietnam started to reform its market in the 1980s, finally reestablishing diplomatic relations with the US.


## Unit 8.3 - Effects of the Cold War
*This demonstrates a major example of the Vietnam as a proxy war between the US and USSR and how the US tried to contain communism.*

**1955** - Eisenhower starts sending military advisers to South Vietnam to train the South Vietnamese Army against North Vietnam. John F. Kennedy increased this amount from 1,000 to 16,000.

##### Problems with Ngo Dinh Diem:
His favoring of Catholicism over Buddhism led to many Buddhist protests.
- The US stopped backing him due to his ineffective leadership, and he was soon assassinated in a US sanctioned coup (1963).

#### The Domino Theory:
- the idea that if one country in the region became communist, other countries would soon follow.
Out of fear that the entire SEA would become communist, Lyndon B. Johnson sends more U.S. troops to South Vietnam in 1964.
