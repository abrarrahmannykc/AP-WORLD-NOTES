    Unit 6.3 Indigenous Responses to State Expansion from 1750 to 1900

    1. The Big Picture: Pushback Across the Globe¶

    As Western powers and Japan expanded their empires (Unit 6.2), indigenous populations did not accept conquest passively. Responses to imperialism varied dramatically, ranging from diplomatic negotiations and religious resistance movements to full-scale military rebellions.

    2. Armed Rebellions in the Americas¶

    As the United States moved West under the banner of "Manifest Destiny," Native American nations fought fiercely to protect their ancestral lands and sovereignty.

    The Cherokee Nation & Political Resistance¶

    • The Strategy: Unlike groups that took up arms immediately, the Cherokee Nation in the southeastern US attempted to use the American legal system. They assimilated to Western cultural standards, adopted a written constitution, and took their grievances to court.

    • The Outcome: In Worcester v. Georgia, the US Supreme Court ruled that Georgia had no right to enforce laws on Cherokee land. However, President Andrew Jackson ignored the ruling, resulting in the forced, brutal relocation of native populations known as the Trail of Tears (1838–1839).

    The Ghost Dance Movement (Late 1800s)¶

    • The Context: Facing confinement on reservations and the deliberate destruction of the buffalo populations, native groups in the Western Plains turned to a spiritual resistance movement.

    • The Belief: Prophesied by a Northern Paiute medicine man named Wovoka, the Ghost Dance was a ritual dance believed to drive out white settlers, return the buffalo herds, and resurrect dead ancestors.

    • The Climax: Terrified by the unifying movement, the US Army attempted to suppress it, leading directly to the Wounded Knee Massacre (1890), which effectively ended armed native resistance on the Plains.

    3. Anti-Colonial Movements in Africa¶

    The Xhosa Cattle-Killing Movement (1856–1857)¶

    • The Context: In South Africa, the Xhosa people were locked in decades of exhausting border wars with British imperial forces. Simultaneously, their livestock was dying from a mysterious European lung disease.

    • The Prophecy: A young Xhosa prophetess named Nongqawuse claimed that spirits told her the people must slaughter all their cattle and destroy their crops. In return, the gods would sweep the British into the sea, bring back healthy cattle, and resurrect dead warriors.

    • The Tragedy: The Xhosa followed the prophecy, killing an estimated 400,000 cattle. Instead of a miracle, it caused a catastrophic self-inflicted famine that killed over 40,000 Xhosa and broke their ability to resist British expansion.

    The War of the Golden Stool (1900)¶

    • The Context: The British Empire wanted to annex the wealthy Asante Kingdom (modern-day Ghana).

    • The Insult: The British governor demanded to sit on the Golden Stool—a sacred object representing the divine spirit, sovereignty, and ancestral soul of the Asante nation that was never allowed to touch the ground.

    • The Resistance: Enraged by the disrespect, an Asante queen mother named Yaa Asantewaa led a massive military rebellion against British forces. Though the British eventually won and annexed the territory, the Asante successfully protected the Golden Stool from capture.

    The Mahdist Revolt in Sudan¶

    • The Context: In East Africa, a charismatic Islamic cleric named Muhammad Ahmad declared himself the Mahdi (the guided one) in 1881.

    • The Rebellion: He launched a fundamentalist holy war to overthrow Egyptian rule and push out British imperial forces. The Mahdist forces famously overran the city of Khartoum. Britain eventually returned years later with advanced Maxim machine guns to reclaim control of Sudan.

    4. Rebellions and New States in Asia¶

    The Indian Rebellion of 1857 (The Sepoy Mutiny)¶

    • The Catalyst: Indian soldiers (Sepoys) rebelled when they discovered their rifle cartridges were greased with cow and pig fat, forcing them to violate both Hindu and Muslim religious dietary laws.

    • The Escalation: The mutiny quickly exploded into a widespread anti-colonial rebellion across northern India, uniting diverse cultural and regional factions against British East India Company rule.

    • The Turning Point: The British state crushed the rebellion, dissolved the East India Company, and placed India under direct crown control (the British Raj), shifting from indirect economic imperialism to direct state governance.

    The Philippine-American War (1899–1902)¶

    • The Betrayal: Filipino nationalists, led by Emilio Aguinaldo, fought alongside the United States during the Spanish-American War to throw off Spanish colonial rule.

    • The Turn: Instead of granting the Philippines independence, the US annexed the islands via the Treaty of Paris. Aguinaldo immediately turned his forces against the US military, leading to a brutal, three-year guerrilla war before American control was consolidated.
