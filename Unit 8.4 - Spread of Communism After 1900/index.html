    Unit 8.4 Spread of Communism After 1900

    Backlink to the entire history of China, which describes: Mao Zedong communist victory five year plans Great Leap Forward Cultural Revolution and Red Guards Punishment of the Red Four ‘Destalinization’ after 1953, China starts establishing relations with US

    We will now discuss land reform movements as a tool for economic change and even political change.

    Iran:¶

    • Britain viewed Persia as a crucial buffer state to protect India during the 19th century, and the weak Qatar Dynasty gave economic abilities to foreign powers (such as Britain gaining control of their oil).
    • Iran’s leader considered support Hitler, which prompted a Russian and British invasion. This led to power being transferred to the leader’s son, Shah Muhammad Reza Pahlavi.
    • 1951 - Nationalists forced Shah Pahlavi to flee the country, and selected Mohammad Mosaddegh as PM in 1953.
      • He wanted to nationalize oil companies, which prompted the US and GB to overthrow Mosaddegh and return the shah to power.
    • The shah ruled harshly under an authoritarian regime, but instituted progressive reforms known as the White Revolution (due to lack of bloodshed).
      • Women suffrage
      • Social welfare
      • Literacy programs
      • Land ownership - The government bought land from landlords and resold to peasants for a cheaper price.
        • Not that effective as not many peasants had this offer available to them.
        • Landowners and frustrated peasants both opposed these land reforms, and religious conservatives opposed modernization, leading to opposition against the Shah.
    • Iranian Revolution (1979):
      • Shah ousted from power
      • Islamic theocracy put into power
      • Government lead by a Guardian Council that interpreted constitution based on shariah law.
      • Also led by a clergy, that could approve/disapprove anyone running for office.
      • Anti-western, anti-Israel

    Venezuela Land-Reforms¶

    • Venezuela sough to end large estate ownership and redistribute lands to peasants.
    • Redistribution of 5 million acres of land: some from state government, some taken from landowners. Problems:
    • lack of support for farmers to actually make the land productive -> abandoned projects
    • some landowners saw this as a violation of human rights
    • illegal squatters sometimes moved in to settle on these lands
    • Political divide: For many Venezuelans, the person they would vote for is based on if they support their point of view on land reforms.

    Guatemala Land-Reforms¶

    Slight Historical Context:¶

    • 1930s - The United Fruit Company (UFCO) controlled almost half of Guatemala’s land, and many of it was unused to prevent other competitors from buying it.
      • They owned rail lines and telegraph systems.
    • The UFCO received lots of land concessions from previous Guatemalan dictators in exchange for low wages.

    Unit 8 Context: - 1954 - Democratically elected President Jacobo Arbenz sought to redistribute this unused land to peasants. - The UFCO then lobbed the US government to remove Arbenz, and the CIA eventually overthrew him. - Results: The land reform was reversed, and revolutionary ideas started spreading, eventually leading to the Guatemalan Civil War (1960-1966).

    Ethiopia Land Reform¶

    • After a struggle against the Italian fascist invasion (1935-1941), Haile Selassie returns to power from exile during WW2.
      • Politically Western alignment
      • Economic success from coffee trade
      • Centralization of power
      • Stronger international affairs
        • Western political and economic reforms
      • Problem: Selassie’s power depended on the support of the land-owning aristocracy and the Ethiopian Orthodox Church. Therefore, he could not implement much land reforms.
      • Military leaders eventually deposed Selassie in 1974, and Mengistu Haile Mariam soon rose to power.
        • Assassinated members of the old regime
        • Established a socialist government, receiving aid from the Soviet Union and other communist countries.
        • Problems: Famine and bad economic policies forced Mengstu to flee to Zimbabwe in 1971.

    India Land Reform¶

    After India’s independence in 1947 and its subsequent partition, India undertook some economic reforms. - Abolished intermediary rent collectors (Zamindari System) - Protected tenants from random evictions - Limits on land a family could own resulted in surplus land distributed across landless farmers, demonstrating cooperative farming India saw mixed results in land reform.

    Kerala¶

    In 1957, Kerala democratically elected a communist government. - 1963: Tenants won the right to purchase land form landowners - 1969: Tenants won the right to become full owners of land - 1974: Laws allowed for minimum wages. The central government was not politically aligned with Kerala’s communist government (as the former sided with landowners), and so they eventually took direct rule of Kerala to reverse land reforms.
