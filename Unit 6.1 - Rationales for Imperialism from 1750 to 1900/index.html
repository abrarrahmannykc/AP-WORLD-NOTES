    Unit 6.1 Rationales for Imperialism from 1750 to 1900

    1. The Big Picture: Why Imperialism?¶

    As the world shifted into the late 19th century, industrialized Western nations (and one notable Asian power, Japan) didn't just casually trade anymore—they wanted complete territorial, political, and economic control over Africa and Asia.

    To justify conquering millions of people, empires built a multi-layered matrix of cultural, nationalistic, and economic rationales.

    2. Cultural Rationales: The Science of Prejudice¶

    Imperial powers twisted science and philosophy to make colonization seem like a charitable, humanitarian act.

    Social Darwinism¶

    • The Theory: Writers took Charles Darwin’s biological theory of evolution ("survival of the fittest") and erroneously applied it to human societies.

    • The Imperialist Logic: They argued that wealthy, technologically advanced white Europeans were naturally superior to other races. Therefore, it was the "natural order" for powerful nations to conquer and displace "weaker" ones.

    📐 Pseudoscientific Racism (Phrenology & Craniometry)¶

    • What it was: Supposed "scientists" traveled the world measuring the sizes and shapes of human skulls.

    • The Bias: They falsely concluded that larger skull sizes among Europeans proved higher intelligence, using this bad science to strip indigenous populations of their self-governing legitimacy.

    The "Civilizing Mission" & Religious Motivations¶

    • The Concept: Celebrated by cultural works like Rudyard Kipling's poem "The White Man's Burden", Europeans claimed they had a moral obligation to civilize non-Western peoples.

    • The Strategy: This meant introducing Western education, medicine, language, and apparel while actively suppressing local cultures.

    • Missionary Network: Thousands of Christian missionaries flooded into Africa and Asia. While they occasionally provided genuine medical care and schools, they acted as a major vanguard for imperial expansion by convincing indigenous populations to abandon traditional religious practices in favor of Western thought.

    3. Nationalistic Rationales:¶

    In the 19th century, size mattered. An empire's global prestige was directly tied to how much colored territory it could carve out on a world map.

    • The British Empire: Driven by intense pride, Great Britain bragged that "the sun never sets on the British Empire," spanning from Canada to India and Australia.

    • The Unification Factor: Newly unified nations like Germany and Italy desperately wanted to prove they were top-tier global powers, aggressively jumping into the race for colonies to match Britain and France.

    • Geopolitics & Naval Bases: Colonies weren't just for prestige; they were strategic military chess pieces. Industrial navies required coaling stations around the globe to refuel warships and guard vital trade bottlenecks (like the newly opened Suez Canal).

    4. Economic Rationales: Industrial Revolution¶

    While cultural and nationalistic ideas were flashy, the absolute primary fuel for imperialism was raw, unfiltered economic demand stemming from the Industrial Revolution.

    • The Supply Problem: Mechanized factories in Europe could produce goods at blistering speeds, but they completely exhausted domestic raw materials. They needed a constant influx of foreign resources to survive.

    • The Target Commodities:

      • Rubber: Extracted from trees in the Congo Basin and Amazon to manufacture tires, hoses, and factory belts.

      • Cotton: Shipped from India and Egypt to feed the textile mills of Manchester.

      • Palm Oil: Sourced from West Africa to lubricate massive, fast-moving factory machinery.

      • Mineral Wealth: Copper (for electrical wiring), gold, and diamonds.

    • Captive Consumer Markets: Industrial nations faced domestic overproduction. To keep profits high, they forced their conquered colonies to buy manufactured Western goods, simultaneously banning local competitive manufacturing (such as destroying India's domestic textile industry).
